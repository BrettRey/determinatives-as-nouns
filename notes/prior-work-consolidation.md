# Prior-Work Consolidation: Determinatives, Pronouns, and the Category/Function Distinction

Prepared for the "Determinatives as nouns" paper. Three short reads, extracting how each source treats (i) determinative-as-category vs determiner-as-function and (ii) pronouns as a type of noun. All three are potential framework-inheritance citations or foils for the superordinate-Noun argument.

---

## Source 1: Reynolds (2013), *TESL Canada Journal* 30(2), 1-17
"Determiners, feline marsupials, and the category-function distinction: A critique of ELT grammars"

**Main claim on category vs function.** The central argument. ELT materials systematically confound the category DETERMINATIVE with the function SPECIFIER (Reynolds's preferred CGEL-era term for what we now call determiner). The paper adopts CGEL's distinction: "I use DETERMINATIVE (like ADJECTIVE) for the category and SPECIFIER (like MODIFIER) for the function, trusting that these two words are distinct enough not to be confused with each other" (p. 5). Three distributional tests define the determinative category: (1) no combination with *the* / *a*, (2) combination with singular count nouns, (3) occurrence in the partitive *Head + of + definite NP* (pp. 7-8). Tables 1 and 2 list the items.

**On pronouns as nouns.** Footnote 1 (p. 15) is the load-bearing quotation for the present paper: "In modern grammars, the pronoun is treated as a subclass of noun rather than as a distinct part of speech. Pronouns are like common and proper nouns in that they occur mainly as head in phrases functioning as subject or object in clause structure." Traditional grammar, by contrast, "did not have a comparable category of NP and focused on the differences between pronouns and common or proper nouns rather than the functional likeness."

**Quotable inheritance.** The *my* set are determinatives (function: specifier), the *mine* set are pronouns (function: head of NP); they are "categories, not functions" (p. 10). Reynolds explicitly notes dual category membership: "we/us and you are primarily pronouns but also exist as determinatives as in *you boys* or *us kids*" (fn. 20). This is the precedent for shape-sharing across the sub-Noun classes the new paper will unify.

## Source 2: Reynolds (2014), *TESL Canada Journal* 31(2), 89-91
"Determinatives again: A response to Lenchuk and Ahmed"

**Main claim on category vs function.** Reynolds restates and defends the 2013 position. The response opens: "The main point of Reynolds (2013) is that a consistent distinction between the category of a word (or any syntactic constituent) and its function provides the flexibility needed to maintain a consistent system of description" (p. 89). Lenchuk and Ahmed, on Reynolds's reading, concede the main point: "Lenchuk and Ahmed affirm the main point: a consistent distinction between function and category should be maintained. ESL grammars don't do this, and they should" (p. 90).

**On pronouns as nouns.** Not directly stated, but the piece contains a formulation that prefigures the "determinatives as nouns" move: the *my* set "meet two criteria for being determinatives," but "there is good reason beyond that to believe that they are nouns" (p. 90). This is a rare explicit claim that a word can belong simultaneously to determinative and noun, and invokes the Aristotelian/HPC point that "the Aristotelian idea of categories being defined by necessary and sufficient characteristics is unworkable (e.g., Lakoff, 1987)."

**Quotable inheritance.** "A failure to recognize that words in different categories may share a shape" is the error Reynolds attributes to opponents (p. 89); "words of different categories may share a shape, just as the noun *try* and the verb *try* do" (p. 90). The *you*-as-determinative/*you*-as-pronoun shape-sharing argument is developed here explicitly. Treat 2014 as the piece where Reynolds first gestures at determinatives being nouns without yet reclassifying them.

## Source 3: Huddleston and Pullum (2005), *A Student's Introduction to English Grammar* (1st ed.), Ch. 5 "Nouns and noun phrases"

**Main claim on category vs function.** The classic SIEG terminological footnote (p. 93, fn. 2) is the single cleanest statement in the English-grammar literature: "We use 'determiner' for the function term and 'determinative' for the category term since the suffix *-er* matches that in the function term 'modifier', while *-ive* matches that in the category term 'adjective'." §3.2 ("Determinatives as modifiers") makes explicit that determinatives can serve functions other than determiner, notably modifier in AdjP/AdvP/NP (example [29]: *all the better*, *that fast*, *its many failings*). §3.3 introduces determinative phrase (DP) as the phrase headed by a determinative, distinct from the function determiner.

**On pronouns as nouns.** Explicit and definitive for the 1st edition taxonomy (p. 84, §1, [8]): "The three major subclasses of noun are **pronouns, proper nouns, and common nouns**." Determinatives are *not* in this list. They are introduced in Ch. 2 as a separate primary lexical category alongside noun, verb, adjective, adverb, preposition. Pronouns are admitted into Noun on distributional grounds: they can head NPs in subject, object, predicative-complement and PP-complement positions ([1]); they differ from other nouns "most clearly by their inability to combine with determiners (cf. **the me, *a myself*)" and by having nominative/accusative case inflection.

**Quotable inheritance.** The SIEG taxonomy is exactly the foil the present paper needs: four candidate subclasses on the table (pronoun, proper, common, determinative), SIEG admits three of them under Noun but treats the fourth as a separate primary category. The paper can cite SIEG's own admission criteria (head of NP in the four major clause-level functions; inability to combine with determiners) and argue that determinatives satisfy them too in their head/fused-head uses (see §7 "fused-head construction," pp. 97ff., which shows determinatives functioning as head of an NP: *any*, *both*, *many*, etc.). The fused-head construction is the distributional hook that lets the superordinate-Noun move not look like pure Abney-ism.

---

**Summary across sources.** Reynolds 2013 hammers the category/function distinction and classes pronouns-as-nouns in a footnote. Reynolds 2014 is the first hint that a word can be both determinative and noun. SIEG 2005 gives the canonical terminology and the three-subclass taxonomy that the new paper will expand to four by adding determinative alongside pronoun, proper, and common under a superordinate Noun node. None of the three sources makes the determinatives-as-nouns move directly; all three supply inheritable framework pieces.

## Source 4: Reynolds (2021), *Cadernos de Linguística* 2(3), e399 — "Quantifying the Differences Between Lexical Categories"

**Main empirical result.** Energy-distance k-groups clustering on a 138-word-form × 232-feature matrix (encoding CGEL features of determinatives and pronouns) produces **93% correspondence with CGEL's categorization**. This is direct empirical support that determinative and pronoun are distinct sub-clusters at the distributional level.

**Interlocutors.** Postal 1966, Sommerstein 1972, Abney 1987, Hudson 2004, Croft 2001, Matthews 2014, Nau 2016 — exactly the opponent set the det-as-noun paper now engages with. Reynolds positions against "methodological opportunism" (Croft 2001) by using a non-parametric statistic that doesn't require choosing criteria a priori.

**Apparent tension with the det-as-noun proposal — resolved.** The paper confirms determinatives and pronouns as **distinct** sub-clusters. The fourfold-Noun taxonomy preserves this distinction at the sub-category level; the supercategory claim is at a higher level (all four project to NP; all four share the Noun-level profile abstractly). Reynolds 2021's result is therefore evidence for the sub-structure the new paper commits to, not against it. The new paper can cite 2021 as "the sub-cluster distance is empirically robust" and then argue for the supercategory on distributional-projection grounds rather than sub-cluster-proximity grounds.

**Quotable inheritance.** The methodological move (binary encoding of CGEL features, energy-distance evaluation) is the empirical base. For the new paper, 2021's message is: the sub-clusters are distinct; the taxonomy has real structure; now we're asking what higher-level category they all belong to.

## Source 5: Huddleston & Pullum 2002 (CGEL) Ch 5 — the framework being revised

**Three-subclass Noun taxonomy**: common, proper, pronoun (CGEL p. 354ff). Determinative is a separate primary lexical category (CGEL Ch 2). The new paper extends the Noun taxonomy to four subclasses by adding determinative.

**Category / function distinction** is canonical in CGEL: "determinative" is the lexical category, "determiner" is the function. The determiner function can be realised by a determinative phrase (DP) or by a genitive NP (CGEL p. 355).

**Fused-head construction** (CGEL §7): determinatives can function as the head of an NP without a common-noun complement (*any, both, many, few, most, such*). The new paper reads fused heads as ordinary nominal headedness rather than as a special fusion construction peculiar to a non-nominal category.

## What the paper inherits (one-page synthesis)

1. **From Reynolds 2013** (TESL): the category/function distinction as the paper's operating framework. Pronouns-as-nouns as the prior move this paper generalises.
2. **From Reynolds 2014** (TESL): the shape-sharing argument ("words of different categories may share a shape"). Critically, the concession that the *my* set "meet two criteria for being determinatives" *and* "there is good reason beyond that to believe that they are nouns" (p. 90). The new paper generalises this gesture from possessives to the whole determinative class.
3. **From Reynolds 2021** (Cadernos): the empirical finding that determinatives and pronouns are distinct sub-clusters. This is *input* to the paper, not a problem for it: the supercategory claim is at a higher level than the sub-cluster distinctness the 2021 clustering recovered.
4. **From Reynolds 2026** (*EL&L*): the numerals absorption (already a nested sub-categorisation within determinative; cardinal nouns splitting into proper/common). Precedent for fine structure within the four sub-clusters.
5. **From CGEL 2002**: the three-subclass Noun taxonomy and the category/function distinction. SIEG 2005 restates this in pedagogical form. The new paper adds the fourth subclass.

**The inheritance story, told compactly for the paper**: CGEL already places pronouns inside Noun despite their morphosyntactic distinctness. Reynolds 2013, 2014, 2021, 2026 develop the category/function apparatus and sharpen the sub-cluster structure. The present paper makes the natural next move in the sequence: determinatives join pronouns, proper nouns, and common nouns as the fourth sub-cluster of Noun.
