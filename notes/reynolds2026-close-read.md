# Reynolds 2026 numerals — close read for det-as-noun paper

**Source:** Reynolds, Brett. 2026. "The lexicon–syntax boundary in English numerals: cardinals, ordinals and fractionals." *English Language and Linguistics*. doi:10.1017/S1360674325100518.

## Bottom line for the det-as-noun paper

The numerals paper already implements, within one narrow domain, exactly the sort of move the det-as-noun paper proposes more broadly. It preserves CGEL's categorial assignments (cardinals as determinatives *and* nouns; ordinals as adjectives; fractionals as nouns) and adds a principled sub-division of cardinal nouns into proper vs. common. **Numerals therefore absorb cleanly into the fourfold-Noun taxonomy without needing a fifth sub-category.**

## §5 Categorial analysis — the anchor passage for citation (p. 10-11)

> "CGEL offers a more principled categorial analysis, consistently distinguishing lexical category from syntactic function. It treats numeratives as clearly defined lexical subcategories: cardinal numeratives are determinatives and nouns, fractionals are nouns, and ordinals are adjectives. I propose a further refinement within the category of cardinal nouns, distinguishing proper nouns (when denoting abstract mathematical entities or serving as labels) from common nouns (when quantifying)."

## Mapping onto the fourfold Noun taxonomy

| Numeral type | Form | Reynolds 2026 category | Fits into Det-as-Noun sub-cluster |
|--------------|------|------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Cardinal | *ten* in *ten men* | Determinative (cardinal) | Determinative sub-cluster |
| Cardinal | *ten* in *Room 101*, *1066* | Proper noun (cardinal) | Proper noun sub-cluster |

Three of the four noun sub-clusters are populated by cardinals alone.

## §5.1 Determinative (cardinal) properties (p. 11)

- "Semantically, they denote counts, quantities, multiples and measures."
- "Syntactically, they head DPs that primarily function as determiners."
- "They license the same dependents as other determinatives (Huddleston & Pullum *et al*. 2002: 431), with the addition of factors, distinguishing them clearly from cardinal nouns."
- "They allow modification by preposition phrases (PPs; e.g.\ *at least twenty people*), other determinative phrases (DPs; e.g.\ *six hundred*) and adverb phrases (AdvPs; e.g.\ *not/almost/exactly/fully twenty people*), a property less typical of cardinal nouns in isolation."
- "Like most determinatives, cardinals don't inflect for number or other grammatical categories."

> Relevance for det-as-noun: the "don't inflect" property is exactly the grammaticalised-end profile that makes the determinative sub-cluster *the defective end* of the Noun supercategory, parallel to auxiliaries as the defective end of Verb.

## Table 1 (p. 12) — Syntactic functions of cardinal numeratives in NPs

| NP function | Determinative | Noun |
|-------------|---------------|------|
| Determiner | *Ten men came.* | *Ten's role varies.* |
| Fused Det-Head | *Ten came.* | *Ten's varies.* |
| Attributive Mod | *The ten men came.* | *The tens column rose.* |
| Fused Mod-Head | *The ten (of them) came.* | *The tens rose.* |
| Head (± plural) | N/A | *I have tens/a ten.* |
| Head (plural ± of PP) | N/A | *I have tens (of pens).* |
| Factor | *Ten thousand is even.* | N/A |

> Relevance for det-as-noun: this table shows that the *same form* alternates across seven syntactic functions depending on whether it is parsed as determinative or noun. In the fourfold taxonomy, this is the kind of polysemy already tolerated inside the supercategory.

## §5.2 Proper nouns (cardinal) (p. 12-13)

> "CGEL categorizes cardinals as both determinatives and nouns, but remains ambiguous regarding their categorization as common or proper nouns. I propose a principled distinction: cardinal nouns are proper nouns when denoting mathematical entities or acting as unique identifiers within quantificational contexts. This duality aligns with systematic noun alternations elsewhere in English (e.g.\ *China*\textsubscript{proper} versus *china*\textsubscript{common})."

Examples (7) where cardinals behave as proper nouns:
- *Room 101 contains our archives.*
- *Take Highway 407 to the airport.*
- *The year 1066 saw the Norman Conquest.*

## §4 Factors as modifiers (p. 9-10) — relevance for stacking/Det-function separation

Reynolds 2026 argues factors (*two* in *two hundred*) are **modifiers** in the NP, not determiners or complements. §4.2 rejects the determiner analysis because:

1. "Determiners are strictly NP-bound in CGEL (Huddleston & Pullum *et al*. 2002: 24). Since numeratives include ordinal adjectives with factors (e.g.\ *two thousandth*), treating factors as determiners would require substantial revisions" (p. 10).
2. "Treating factors as determiners necessitates new machinery to account for the lack of number agreement: \**the year two thousands and five*." (p. 10)
3. Fractionals "require a determiner such as *seven*, despite the presence of the factor *three*. And the number of the final word agrees with the determiner: *one two-hundredth, two two-hundredths*" (p. 10).
4. "If the factor were a determiner, the NP would contain two determiners, something explicitly rejected by *CGEL*" (p. 10).

> Relevance for det-as-noun: the last point is the same argument Brett already has — stacking is barred *functionally* (one Determiner per NP), not *categorially*. Reynolds 2026 treats this as a given; the det-as-noun paper can cite it.

## Figure 3 (p. 12) — Lexical category structure

```
Determinatives
├── cardinal
│   ├── extended basic
│   │   ├── zero
│   │   ├── one_s
│   │   └── basic
│   │       ├── single-digit (one_n–nine)
│   │       ├── teen (10–19)
│   │       ├── decade (20, 30, …, 90)
│   │       └── compound (21–29, …, 91–99)
│   └── magnitude (hundred, thousand, million, …)
└── others
```

> Relevance: this is fine-grained sub-categorisation *within* the determinative class. It demonstrates that "determinative" is not a flat label — the sub-structure extends downward. The det-as-noun paper extends the sub-categorisation upward: determinative is itself a sub-category of Noun.
