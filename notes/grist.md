# Grist — Determinatives as nouns

Raw accumulating notes. Tidy later.

## Core move

Superordinate category **Noun**, with four sub-categories: common noun, proper noun, pronoun, determinative. Not Abney's DP. Category / function distinction preserved.

## What makes them all nouns (candidate shared profile)

- Head nominal expressions (NPs)
- Combine with determiners (at least some, in at least some contexts)
- Bear case in languages with overt case
- Serve as subject / object / complement of P
- Referential / quantificational / deictic function
- Number and gender (where applicable) morphology or at least agreement triggers

Needs unpacking: which of these are shared across all four sub-clusters, and which are sub-cluster-specific?

## Distinguishing the four sub-clusters

| Sub-cluster | Profile | Stabilising mechanisms |
|-------------|---------|------------------------|
| Common noun | Lexically rich, open class, takes determiners, takes adjectival modification | Acquisition pressure, lexical learning |
| Proper noun | Designates individual; restricted determiner use; doesn't pluralise in the neutral way | Naming conventions, reference tracking |
| Pronoun | Deictic / anaphoric; closed class; person/number/gender features; restricted determiner-taking | Paradigm maintenance, discourse conventions |
| Determinative | Functions as determiner in NP; some fused-head use (e.g. *some, this*); closed class | Grammaticalisation, functional-word entrenchment |

## Objections / things to address

1. **The distributional-distance problem.** Reynolds 2021 (Cadernos) found pronouns and determinatives pattern very differently. How can they both be nouns if they're that far apart? Answer probably: HPC profile at the superordinate level + distinct sub-cluster mechanisms. The profile is *what's shared*, not *everything*.
2. **Why not treat them as separate categories?** CGEL does. What does the proposed unification buy us? Likely answers: predictive purchase for acquisition, typological comparison, semantic-syntactic coupling.
3. **The pronoun / determinative overlap.** Some items (*this, some, any, all, both*) can be either. On the four-sub-cluster view, is that syncretism or true category ambiguity? (Connects to Reynolds 2021 full-matrix work.)
4. **Fused-head constructions.** *I'll take some* — if *some* is fused-head determinative, that's a determinative functioning as head of NP (without a complement), which pushes on determinative-as-noun elegantly. Might be a cleaner argument than a distributional one.
5. **Cross-linguistic.** Is this proposal claiming Noun-with-four-subtypes universally, or just for English? (CGEL's cross-linguistic tolerance matters.) Also relevant: Wiltschko's *Universal Structure*.

The proposal here should either absorb numerals into the Noun taxonomy or give a principled reason why they stay out.
