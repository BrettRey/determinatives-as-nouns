# *Word Structure* — predicted reviewer response and revision strategy

Brett's prediction (2026-04-29) of how a *Word Structure* reviewer who knows Payne, Huddleston & Pullum 2010 would respond to the current draft, with revision priorities. Predicted decision: **major revisions with a real path to acceptance**, not desk reject and not easy accept.

Source for PHP 2010 (markdown + PDF): `literature/Payne, Huddleston, Pullum - 2010 - The distribution and category status of adjectives and adverbs(2) copy.md`.

## Why PHP 2010 is the load-bearing precedent

PHP 2010 is explicitly about distribution and category status: it attacks a received category claim, defines distributional cores, tests them in a wider range of environments, and separates distributional evidence from word-class ontology. That is structurally what this paper wants to be. PHP 2010 also already discusses *any*, *some*, *many*, *few*, *nothing*, *anybody* in exactly the area the paper targets, and explicitly treats them as determinatives in fused determiner-head function (paralleling *best* as adjective in *The best are here*). So the paper is not analogous to PHP 2010, it is a sequel that revises one of its categorial-architecture assumptions.

## Three places PHP 2010 will be used against the paper

### 1. Direct sequel framing

The paper currently cites Payne et al. 2010 only in connection with adjective/adverb distribution and determinative function. PHP 2010 should be promoted to a major methodological foil. The framing should be:

> Payne, Huddleston & Pullum were right that independent *few*, *any*, *some*, etc. should not be recategorized as pronouns or common nouns. The present proposal preserves that insight, but relocates the determinative category itself inside noun.

This stops the reviewer reading the paper as "undoing PHP 2010."

### 2. Distribution per se

PHP 2010 ends by warning that distribution alone does not settle category ontology, then appeals to conceptual, semantic, morphological, and lexical evidence to defend adverb as a distinct major category. A PHP-minded reviewer will ask why projection/distribution justifies reducing determinative to noun here, when PHP 2010 insists distributional overlap between adjectives and adverbs does not justify a single category.

The answer needs to be sharper than "because determinatives occur where nouns occur." It is:

- determinatives share the **NP-projection profile** of the noun supercategory;
- they differ from common nouns, proper nouns, and pronouns in ways that are already tolerated among noun sub-categories;
- the category reduction simplifies an independently problematic phrase-type disjunction at the determiner slot;
- the proposal preserves determinative as a real sub-category, rather than collapsing it into pronoun or common noun;
- the closed-class/defective behaviour of articles and quantifiers is subcategory-internal, not evidence for a separate primary category.

The contrast that needs to be stated explicitly:

> PHP 2010 rejects a category merger for adjectives and adverbs because the shared environments do not correspond to a shared projectional, paradigmatic, semantic, and morphological supercategory. The determinative case is different because the proposed merger is not between coordinate major categories but between a closed nominal sub-category and a broader noun supercategory whose existing members already vary sharply in morphology, semantics, modification, and determination.

This is probably the single most important argumentative addition.

### 3. Adverb-modifying-noun reintroduction

PHP 2010 uses the determinative analysis partly to protect its account of adjective/adverb distribution: if *any*, *some*, *nothing* etc. were pronouns, *hardly any*, *precisely nothing*, *almost anybody* would look like adverbs modifying pronouns (hence nouns). PHP 2010 avoids that by keeping them determinatives. If determinatives are nouns, the problem reappears.

§7 anticipates this by treating AdvP modification as a sub-cluster property of determinative-headed NPs, vs. AdjP modification of common-noun-headed NPs. The response is right but needs to be more prominent and explicitly tied to PHP 2010. The reviewer needs to be persuaded that "noun" in the proposal is not equivalent to "common noun", so *hardly any* is not a counterexample to the adjective/adverb generalization for common nouns; it is evidence that determinative-nouns have their own internal modifier profile. The proposal does not predict *genuine any*, *experienced every*, or *almost dog*.

## Likely reviewer praise

- Clear *Word Structure* problem: category status, not an isolated construction. PHP 2010's title could almost be a template.
- Preserves a lot of CGEL: keeps category/function distinction, keeps N-headed NP, keeps determinative as descriptively real, allows fusion to remain for some cases.
- Three discriminators in §4.1 (paradigm productivity, structural saturation, internal modification) are reviewer-friendly and concrete.
- Article section is ambitious: defectiveness, grammaticalization, and category membership all linked.

## Likely reviewer objections

1. **The paper has not yet shown determinatives are nouns; it has shown some determinatives can serve as NP pivots or fused heads.** PHP 2010 already handles *few* in *Few are here* as determinative-in-fused-head. The reply (simple cases like *Some left*, *Many came*, *All agree* are structurally saturated and productively distributed enough to be ordinary Head rather than fused Det–Head) may still read as preference for parsimony rather than forced conclusion.

2. **Articles are category-diagnostic counterexamples, not just defective.** If the most frequent and central determinatives fail the nounhood criterion, the criterion is not really a category criterion. Need a stronger, less apologetic response: *the* and *a* are high-frequency grammaticalized endpoints, not central in the category-theoretic sense; demonstratives and quantifiers carry the productive category profile.

3. **Internal modification evidence may be too narrow.** *The lucky few*, *the chosen few*, *the remaining three*, *the selected two* are suggestive, but a skeptic may ask whether *few* is being reanalysed locally as a common noun ('small number'), or whether numerals are special. Need a broader inventory of determinatives that occur as modified independent heads, or a clearer reason why numerals and *few* aren't exceptional.

4. **Lean appendix is not doing enough.** Appendix itself says it isn't an empirical proof, just an architectural audit trail. For a first submission, consider removing or reducing to a brief supplementary note, since a reviewer already processing a bold category claim doesn't need a second methodological novelty.

## Imagined decision letter

> This is an original and provocative paper that falls squarely within the journal's interest in word classes and category status. Its closest precedent is Payne, Huddleston & Pullum's 2010 *Word Structure* article on adjectives and adverbs, and the present paper is potentially a valuable extension of that line of work to determinatives. The paper's major contribution is to argue that determinatives should be retained as a coherent class but analysed as a sub-category of noun. The argument is promising, especially in its distinction between simple independent cases and the residual Mod–Head/fused-head cases. However, the paper needs to engage more directly with Payne et al. 2010, since that article explicitly treats *few*, *any*, *nothing*, and related forms as determinatives in fused-head structures. The author should clarify why the proposed noun-supercategory analysis does not undermine Payne et al.'s account of adverbial modification of determinatives, and why the evidence favours canonical Head over fused Det–Head rather than merely permitting it. The treatment of articles as defective members also requires further defence. I recommend major revision.

## Concrete revision: add a "Payne–Huddleston–Pullum precedent" subsection

Probably in §6 or at the end of §1. Three points:

1. PHP 2010 establishes *Word Structure* as appropriate venue for English category-status arguments built from distributional and morphosyntactic diagnostics.
2. PHP 2010's determinative analysis is not being rejected wholesale. Accept their anti-pronoun move: *few*, *any*, *some*, *nothing* should not be scattered across pronoun and determinative depending on whether a following common-noun head is present.
3. The disagreement is one level higher: PHP 2010 treats determinative as a primary category; this paper treats it as a sub-category of noun. Frame as **preserving PHP's local categorial continuity while revising the supercategory assignment**.

## Bottom line

With PHP 2010 in view, a reviewer would regard the paper as **very much in scope**. The likely criticism is not "wrong journal" but "you are challenging the machinery of a landmark *Word Structure* paper and need to say so more directly." Major revisions with a real path to acceptance, provided the revised version foregrounds the PHP 2010 comparison and clarifies why the determinative–noun case is not vulnerable to PHP's warning that distribution alone cannot decide category status.
