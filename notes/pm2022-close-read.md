# Pullum & Miller (2022), "NPs versus DPs: Why Chomsky was right" — close read of pp. 6-22

## 1. What P&M concede

**Determinatives *can* function as attributive modifiers.** Commenting on Abney's example *the duke's every wish*, P&M write: "*X's every N* is actually a frozen archaic construction inherited from a previous stage of the language where *every* could be used as an attributive modifier, as is possible in Present-Day English for several determinatives, including *several*, *many*, and *few*. These can readily appear in attributive modifier position following a definite article, as in *the few/many/several wishes that the duke made*" (p. 13).

**Some "one-word substantive phrases" consist of just a determinative.** "setting aside anaphoric omission of repeated nouns, we find substantive phrases like *this* or *some* which consist of a determinative and nothing else" (p. 13). They immediately defang this with five points: articles *a/an*, *the* never stand alone (p. 13, [i]); nounless substantive phrases are under 5% of running text while determinative-less ones are over 50% ([ii]); the traditional analysis would call these fused-head uses, treating the determinatives as homonymous pronouns ([iii]); headlinese drops determinatives as if redundant ([iv]); head nouns can be inserted into most nounless NPs ([v]) but determinatives cannot be inserted into phrases like *your house*.

**Genitive NPs fill the determiner position.** They present structure (14b) where *Albert Einstein's* (a DP in Abney's analysis but a genitive NP for them) occupies the specifier/determiner slot before an empty D, noting "For cases of the second type, as in (14b), it is much more difficult to argue in favor of the existence of a D position between the genitive determiner and the noun" (p. 12).

**They concede the complementizer-as-head analysis is defensible for clauses** (while not advocating it): "the choice of the complementizer and the form of the head verb are governed by mutual constraints, allowing the widely accepted analysis where the complementizer is the morphosyntactic head (though we ourselves do not advocate this analysis; see footnote 16 above)" (p. 18).

**They concede Jackendoff-style NP-internal structure.** Their reference tree (8) follows Chomsky/Harris X-bar where "the label N'' ('N double bar') corresponds to 'NP', and N' ('N bar'), an intermediate constituent embracing N and its complements and modifiers, excluding the determinative. (The N' is called a 'nominal' by CGEL and some others.)" (p. 7). The determinative sits outside N' but inside N''.

## 2. What P&M deny

They deny that D (the determinative) is the head of the substantive phrase. The core argument: "The central motivation seems to spring solely from considerations of theoretical elegance, as seen in the light of internal developments within Chomskyan generative grammar" (p. 10), i.e. structural parallelism with IP/CP, not empirical force. They deny there are "a significant number of strong empirical arguments in favor of the DP analysis" (p. 10).

They deny the empty-D move for determinative-less NPs: "Abney and other proponents of the DP analysis are forced to propose structures with a D head that has no phonetic, phonological, morphological, syntactic or semantic content" (p. 12). Cross-linguistically, "numerous languages (most Slavic languages, Chinese, Japanese, Latin, etc.) do not have articles... The DP analysis consequently leads one to assume that in these languages the great majority of occurrences of substantive phrases have an empty determinative as head, which seems unparsimonious to say the least" (p. 14).

They deny Abney's claim that personal pronouns are determinatives because they can't follow an article (*\*the she*): they adduce COCA examples *the me I am now*, *the her who needs sex*, *the her we thought she'd be* (p. 20, ex. 27).

They deny that Stowell's PRO-in-NP argument has substance: "a null and meaningless D node can always be posited to serve as the required D, his claim has no real substance" (p. 18).

## 3. Article-specific material

**Articles never stand alone.** "Almost every noun can serve as a substantive phrase on its own, but the most central and stereotypical determinatives, namely the articles *a\~an* and *the*, never can" (p. 13, point [i]).

**The head noun governs the choice of determinative, not the reverse.** "In the case of the substantive phrase, it is immediately obvious that it is the choice of the head noun that governs the form of the determinative, and not the contrary. As shown in (19), the count or non-count status of the head noun constrains the types of determinatives with which it can combine" (p. 16). Examples: *some/not much/\*a sodium* vs *a/\*some/\*not much table*.

**Singular count nouns require a determinative; plural count and non-count do not.** "the properties of the noun determine whether it can appear without a determinative: singular count nouns require a determinative whereas plural count and non-count do not" (p. 16, ex. 20): *I want a sandwich* / *\*I want sandwich* / *I want some sandwiches* ~ *I want sandwiches* / *I want some beer* ~ *I want beer*.

**Common vs proper status governs article choice.** "the presence and choice of a determinative is governed by the common vs. proper status of the head noun, and in the case of proper nouns, by the specific subclass" (p. 16, ex. 21): *I see the/\*Ø man*, *I see \*the/Ø Kim*, *I like \*the/Ø France*, *The/\*Ø Thames is lovely*.

**Nouns don't morphologically adapt to determinatives.** "nouns do not have a variety of forms allowing them to adapt to the choice of a given determiner, so that most nouns would be classified as 'defective' from this perspective. This is implausible" (p. 17). Contrast with verbs, which do have forms (infinitive, present participle, past participle) to satisfy auxiliaries.

## 4. Positive arguments for N-headedness

**Zwicky's two morphosyntactic criteria**, applied systematically in §4:

**(i) Subcategorisand criterion** (p. 8): "if, in a construction, one item governs the form of another, the former is the head and the latter the dependent." Applied in §4.2: the noun governs the determinative (count/non-count, singular/plural, common/proper — all examples above), so N is head. Conversely "the determinative [does not] impose extrinsic constraints on the noun. The subcategory to which a noun belongs (count, non-count, proper, or whatever) is an intrinsic property of the noun and is linked to its lexical semantics" (p. 17).

**(ii) Morphosyntactic locus criterion** (p. 8): "the item within a constituent where a property imposed by an external governor is morphosyntactically marked" is the head. Applied in §4.1 (p. 14-16): governing verbs do not select determinatives on their objects — "There are no verbs in English or French that require a direct object having a demonstrative or a definite article as determinative" (p. 16). But verbs *do* require their object to have a noun as head: "the governing verb does impose a morphosyntactic constraint on its direct object, namely that it must have a noun as its head, rather than some other category (compare *We devoured much meat* with *\*We devoured very meaty*)" (p. 16). So the noun, not the determinative, is the morphosyntactic locus.

**Selectional restrictions track N, not D** (Abney concedes this, p. 20): "An obvious objection to the DP-analysis is that unlike C and I, D does not appear to be selected by a matrix head; but as is well-known, selectional restrictions *are* imposed on N. This would argue against D as the head of the noun phrase" (quoting Abney 1987: 85-86).

**Summary of the asymmetry**: "In the substantive phrase, the noun imposes its intrinsic morphosyntactic properties on the elements that surround it, suggesting that it is indeed the noun that is the head of the construction, so it is an NP rather than a DP" (p. 18).

**Agreement within the NP tracks the noun, not the determinative.** The Abney tree in (29) for *those terrible Moscow winters* generates *those*_pl but the nearest NP is *Moscow*_sg; agreement has to reach past *Moscow* to *winters*: "The number properties of the plural determinative *those* are selected by the noun *winters*... How then can one ensure that the determinative will agree with the right noun?" (p. 22).

**Attributive adjectives problem for DP** (p. 21, Abney's tree 28): under Abney, D selects AdjP and Adj selects NP. But adjectives are inert for count/number features, so the tree cannot explain why *\*an interesting information*, *\*an interesting men* are bad while *an interesting man* is fine. "If Adj has no features for number and count status, how can it select the following noun?" (p. 21).
