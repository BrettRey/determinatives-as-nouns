# Abney 1987 as foil

**Source:** Abney, Steven P. 1987. *The English Noun Phrase in its Sentential Aspect*. PhD thesis, MIT. Thesis supervisor: Richard Larson; committee: Chomsky, Hale, Kayne. Dedicated to his wife Nina.

## Core DP hypothesis statement (Abstract)

> "This dissertation is a defense of the hypothesis that the noun phrase is headed by a functional element (i.e., 'non-lexical' category) D, identified with the determiner. In this way, the structure of the noun phrase parallels that of the sentence, which is headed by Infl(ection), under assumptions now standard within the Government-Binding (GB) framework."

## The analogy Abney uses — and why it is the *opposite* of the one Brett uses

> "To make these results fully general, we are led to treat the remaining syncategorematic elements — in particular, determiners in noun phrases and degree words in adjective phrases — as heads of full phrases. The analogy with complementizers and modals indicates that determiners and degree words should head noun phrases and adjective phrases, respectively. In other words, **determiners are lexical instantiations of 'D' in the same way that modals are lexical instantiations of Infl**." (Abstract; emphasis added)

**Critical observation for Brett's paper.** Abney's argument relies on modals being heads of Infl — a non-lexical functional category above VP. The Pullum & Wilson 1977 / GPSG / CGEL tradition rejects this move for auxiliaries: modals and auxiliaries are verbs (a sub-cluster of V). Brett's determinative-as-Noun proposal extends this same tradition to determinatives. **Abney and Brett therefore share the same structural analogy (determinatives::modals) but draw opposite conclusions, because they start from opposite premises about modals.**

Under Abney's premise (modals = Infl = functional head), determinatives must also be functional heads (D), yielding the DP analysis. Under Pullum & Wilson / CGEL's premise (modals = V = sub-cluster of a substantive category), determinatives are also a sub-cluster of a substantive category (N), yielding the fourfold-Noun analysis.

## Abney's concession that P&M 2022 quote against him (p. 85-86)

> "An obvious objection to the DP-analysis is that unlike C and I, D does not appear to be selected by a matrix head; but as is well-known, selectional restrictions *are* imposed on N. This would argue against D as the head of the noun phrase." (Abney 1987: 85-86, quoted in Pullum & Miller 2022: 20)

## Current status

Chomsky 2020 explicitly retracts the DP hypothesis: "I'm going to assume here that nominal phrases are actually NPs. The DP hypothesis, which is widely accepted, was very fruitful, leading to a lot of interesting work; but I've never really been convinced by it. I think these structures are fundamentally nominal phrases. [...] As far as determiners are concerned, like say *that*, I suspect that they are adjuncts. So I'll be assuming that the core system is basically nominal" (Chomsky 2020, quoted in P&M 2022: 4-5).

## Treatment in the paper

One paragraph, read as follows:

> Abney (1987) argues that the noun phrase is headed by a functional element D, parallel to the Infl-headed clause. This DP hypothesis became widely assumed in generative syntax and has been defended by Bernstein (2001), Larson (2019), and others. The present paper does not adopt it. The DP analysis and the fourfold-Noun analysis share an underlying structural analogy — determinatives are to nouns what modals are to verbs — but Abney treats modals as heads of a functional category (Infl) and accordingly treats determinatives as heads of a functional category (D). The Pullum & Wilson (1977) and CGEL tradition treats modals as a sub-category of verb; the present paper extends the same move to determinatives. Chomsky (2020) now rejects the DP hypothesis. Further arguments against DP are in Payne (1993), Bruening (2009, 2020), Pullum & Miller (2022); these are not rehearsed here.

## Note: the *modal / determinative* analogy goes both ways

This is a finding worth flagging for the paper. Abney *uses* the modal-determinative analogy to argue for DP (treating modals and determinatives symmetrically as functional heads). Brett's paper can use the *same* analogy against DP (treating modals and determinatives symmetrically as substantive sub-categories). The empirical facts Abney relies on (possessor agreement in some languages, parallelism with CP) don't decide the symmetry direction. What decides it is whether you treat auxiliaries / modals as a sub-category of Verb (Pullum & Wilson, CGEL) or as heads of a separate functional category Infl (GB / Minimalism). Once auxiliaries-as-V is adopted, determinatives-as-N is the consistent sister move. That is the argument Brett is making.
