## (i) Fused-head constructions

**Current CGEL treatment.** CGEL Ch 5 §9 (pp. 410-422) sets up the fused-head construction as a specific deviation from the canonical NP pattern "noun as head, alone or accompanied by dependents" (CGEL: 325). The head is "realised jointly with a dependent function" (CGEL: 410), and three subtypes are distinguished structurally: (a) simple fused-heads (*two [boys]*), (b) partitive fused-heads (*most of the boys*), (c) special fused-heads (*the rich*, *many*, *the unobtainable*). Example [12] on p. 410 gives *[the rich]* (modifier-head), *[Many] would agree* (determiner-head), *[double]* (predeterminer-head). Tree diagrams (CGEL: 412) label the relevant node `Det-Head: D` or `Mod-Head: AdjP`, and Payne, Huddleston & Pullum 2007 make fusion a first-class theoretical construct (FF, "fusion of functions"), defined by six properties (2007: 11-12, our (39)) — adjacency, single-category realization, non-identity of Y(P) and XP, and so on. Crucially, CGEL is explicit that fusion is *not* ellipsis: "we analyse the construction in terms of fusion of the head with a dependent function rather than in terms of ellipsis of the head" (CGEL: 410, fn. 3).

**The data.** CGEL gives the inventory: demonstratives (*those cards are Kim's*), universals (*all/both (of them)*), cardinals (*two played tennis*), most of the rest of the determinatives (*some*, *any*, *several*, *few*, *enough*, *sufficient*, *more*, *less*, *another*, *either*, *neither*, *no/none*, *one*). *The* and *a* are the conspicuous exceptions: "Unlike most determinatives, *the* is completely excluded from fused-head function" (CGEL: 371, [8]), and "*A* is found only in determiner function. It is thus not used in partitives or other fused-head NPs: this gap is filled by *one*" (CGEL: 372, on [10]).

**Det-as-noun redescription.** On the present proposal, there is no "fusion" at all in the central cases. *Some* in *I'll take [some]* is simply a nominal head filling the Head function of an NP, exactly as *dogs* does in *I'll take [dogs]*. "Determinative" is a noun subcategory, so a determinative-headed NP is no more marked than a pronoun-headed NP or a common-noun-headed NP. The apparatus of fusion (adjacency conditions, dual function assignment, the single-mother complication Payne et al. 2007: 6 acknowledge) is unnecessary. Even the compound-determinative analysis of *somebody, someone, once, twice* (PHP 2007) reduces to the familiar "noun head + dependent" schema. What remains to be explained is the *defectiveness* of *the/a* (only), which becomes a property of a lexical sub-cluster rather than a general fact about the category.

**Strong/weak payoff.** **Very strong.** Fusion-of-functions is the most theoretically costly machinery in Payne & Huddleston's (2002) NP chapter; eliminating it across the simple and partitive cases simplifies the analysis substantially while keeping CGEL's category/function distinction intact. It also dissolves the exceptionalism of CGEL's claim (CGEL: 410, fn. 3) that "one other construction, a minor one, where we recognise an NP that does not have a noun as head." On the det-as-noun proposal, it *does* have a noun as head (where "noun" is superordinate). This is the payoff argument most directly continuous with PHP 2007's own rhetoric about parsimony.

## (ii) Stacking restriction ("one Determiner per NP")

**Current CGEL treatment.** CGEL doesn't state stacking as a theorem, but it's implicit in the phrase-structure diagrams: a standard NP has one Det slot. However, Ch 5 §7 documents quite a bit of evidence against a strict *categorial* stacking ban. *The many people*, *the few people*, *his every whim* and similar are discussed at various points. Lyons (1999: 44) gives the same examples: "Possessives, however, follow *the*, but only with a possessive first-person partitive complement... *his every whim, the king's every whim*." And the CGEL discussion of *all* and *both* (p. 373-5) distinguishes their predeterminer use (*all the students*) from their determiner use (*all students*) — which already requires two slots. The 2007 paper also uses the label "DP" for *almost every* phrases in determiner function (PHP 2007: 3, fn. 2), with *every* as head, *almost* as modifier — so the NP has only one Det, but that Det is a phrase containing multiple determinative lexemes.

**The data.** The key empirical point is that sequences that look like "two determinatives" are easy to find: *the many people*, *the few wishes*, *the several proposals*, *these three cats*, *his every whim*, *all those books*, *the one student*. These are uncontroversially grammatical. CGEL handles them variously by predeterminer slots, modifier function, or DP-internal modification, depending on case.

**Det-as-noun redescription.** Restate the generalization *functionally*: one Det-function slot per NP. Under the det-as-noun proposal this is not ad hoc: it's a specification on a functional slot (which every category occupies in exactly one instance per NP, just like one Head per NP), orthogonal to what lexical category fills it. *The many people* then has *the* in Det function and *many* in Mod function, both realised by determinatives (now a subcategory of noun). No categorial stacking ban is needed. Compare: *that stupid idiot* has two nouns in the NP (*idiot* Head, *stupid* Mod) — nobody bats an eye. Similarly *the many people* has two determinatives-i.e.-nouns in the NP, *people* Head, *many* Mod, *the* Det.

**Strong/weak payoff.** **Moderately strong, less dramatic than (i).** The move here is mainly expository/formalisation-level: the generalization was always functional (since CGEL already allows *many* as modifier in *the many people*), but det-as-noun makes the functional statement *natural* rather than stipulative. It also explains why possessives (plain NPs) and determinative phrases can both fill Det — they're both nominal. Weaker than (i) because CGEL already handles these cases without fusion machinery; the payoff is cleanness rather than new analytical power.

## (iii) NP:Det uniformity

**Current CGEL treatment.** In CGEL (Ch 5 §4 and §5), the Det function is filled by either (a) a determinative or DP (*the*, *this*, *every*, *almost every*), or (b) a genitive NP (*John's*, *the student's*, *that guy over there's*). Payne, Huddleston & Pullum 2007: 2 are explicit: "the determiner is characteristically realized by a determinative such as *the, a, this, some, any, one, two*, etc., [but] it can also be realized by NPs – usually genitive, as in *this guy's shoes*, but sometimes plain, as in *what size shoes*." Lyons 1999: 23 independently notes: "possessive forms of noun phrases occur as modifying expressions within other noun phrases" (his (67)), including *[[my] cousin], [[Fred's] only friend], [[that man next door's] car]*. CGEL's tree representation thus has the Det slot filled by either a DP or an NP — a category mismatch at the slot level.

**The data.** Minimal pairs: *the dog* (Det=DP), *John's dog* (Det=NP), *that man next door's dog* (Det=NP containing PP). All three phrases have *dog* as ultimate head; all three are definite NPs. The genitive NPs themselves have their own internal structure — *that man next door's* is a full NP with *man* as head, *that* as Det, *next door* as modifier. Yet it fills a Det slot in the outer NP.

**Det-as-noun redescription.** If determinatives are nouns, and the outer "DP" filling Det function (e.g., *almost every* in *almost every problem*) is actually an NP, then the Det function is always filled by an NP. Uniformity at the slot level. No longer does CGEL need to say "Det is filled by DP or NP (with special conditions on the latter)"; instead Det is filled by NP, and genitive morphology is a matter that applies to any NP filling Det, regardless of its lexical head.

**Strong/weak payoff.** **Strong but perhaps the most technical.** The uniformity argument has real formal bite — eliminating a category disjunction at a functional slot is non-trivial — but it's the argument that will be least visible to non-syntacticians. It pairs naturally with (i): both are "clean up Det function" moves. The risk: some readers will find it a change of label without empirical consequence. Need to show a consequence (e.g., something about determiner-genitive interaction, or about why both types of Det share properties).

## (iv) Attributive-modifier use of determinatives

**Current CGEL treatment.** CGEL treats several determinatives as capable of modifier function within Nom. *The many people*, *the few wishes*, *the several proposals*, *the one student* all have *many/few/several/one* as modifier (not as a different category). CGEL Ch 5 §7 on cardinal numerals (p. 385-6): "cardinal numerals are determiners in *one time, two times*, but modifiers in *that one time, the two times I tried it myself*." PHP 2007: 3 make the same point explicitly: "the cardinal numeral determinatives are determiners in *one time, two times*, but modifiers in *that one time, the two times I tried it myself*." And PHP 2010: 13-14 explicitly generalize: "few is a determinative in [13bi] as well as [13ai]... the items concerned take the same modifiers in the two constructions." So the determinative *few* in *few doctors* (Det function) and *few of the doctors* (fused-head) and *the few doctors* (Mod function) is the same lexeme in three functions — paralleling the adjective *best* in the same three slots.

**The data.** PHP 2010: 13, examples (13): *[Few doctors] are here* vs *[The best doctors] are here* vs *[Few (of the doctors)] are here* vs *[The best (of the doctors)] are here*. The structural parallel between determinative *few* and adjective *best* is presented as a *good* thing — same function alternation, same category throughout. Also *the many people*, *the several submissions* — all determinative-as-modifier.

**Det-as-noun redescription.** Determinatives-as-nouns neutralises the oddity that a member of category D can appear in a function (Mod) that is otherwise dominated by adjectives. On the det-as-noun view, determinatives are like any noun-class that shows up in modifier function (cf. attributive common nouns: *stone wall*, *student teacher*). The four subcategories of noun — common, proper, pronoun, determinative — each have their own profile of functions they can fill, and some overlap in modifier function. Critically, adverbs can modify determinatives (*almost every*, *very nearly all*, *hardly any*; PHP 2010: 14, example (12)) but adjectives modify nouns (*a professional*, *a genuine professional*). If determinatives are a subtype of noun, the adverb-facts in *almost every, hardly any* look prima facie anomalous (you'd expect adjective modification like *\*genuine every*) — which means (iv) is NOT a straightforward payoff: it *creates* a problem about pronoun/determinative modifier-selection. PHP 2010: 13-14 use the adverb-facts as evidence FOR the determinative analysis and AGAINST pronoun analysis; the det-as-noun paper has to explain how these adverb-facts survive even under a superordinate-Noun view.

**Strong/weak payoff.** **Moderate to weak as a load-bearing payoff.** The attributive-modifier use is already handled by CGEL (it's not an unsolved problem). More importantly, the paper will have to engage with the asymmetry: determinatives take adverb modifiers (*hardly any*), common nouns take adjective modifiers (*a genuine professional*). That asymmetry is evidence for a *sub-cluster* distinction within Noun, not against the superordinate Noun. So this phenomenon, properly framed, *supports* the "Noun has sub-profiles" HPC reading, but it's not a silver bullet. The slogan "same lexeme, two functions" is a good one but *best* and *professional* show that adjectives already do this — so it's not unique to the det-as-noun proposal.

## Steelman need: the articles *the* and *a/an*

**Current CGEL treatment.** *The* and *a* are "the two articles" (CGEL: 368) and the core members of the determinative category. CGEL explicitly notes their defectiveness: "Unlike most determinatives, *the* is completely excluded from fused-head function" (CGEL: 371, [8]: *\*Get rid of [the] immediately!*). "*A* is found only in determiner function" (CGEL: 372). So *the/a* never stand alone as NPs; they lack the fused-head life that all other determinatives have. They also have the most restricted modifier distribution and the most semantically bleached content (Lyons 1999: 36 characterizes them as "the basic, unmarked... with their minimal semantic content ([+ Def] and [+ Sg] respectively), reflected in their phonological weakness"). Payne, Huddleston & Pullum 2007: 22-23 also note that "the determinative *every* cannot occur as fused determiner-head (*\*every of the students*), and the determinative *no* has a specialized morphological variant *none* which is restricted to the fused function" — so defectiveness is a lexical property of specific determinatives, not a category-level generalisation.

**Lyons's picture.** Lyons 1999 builds a sustained argument that *the/a* are the **basic/unmarked** definite and cardinal determiners, phonologically weak, semantically minimal (1999: 36), and diachronically reduced from fuller demonstratives and numerals. He argues articles typically arise from grammaticalisation of demonstratives (Lyons 1999: 116 "definite articles in nearly all languages that have them are descended historically from demonstratives. It is in fact usually a deictically unmarked demonstrative, or a non-proximal or non-first-person one"; also 1999: 322-ff). *A* is "derived historically from the same ancestral form as the numeral *one*, and fewer writers now recognize a synchronic link" (Lyons 1999: 34). This makes *the/a* the *reduced* ends of two determinative continua: definite (demonstratives) and cardinal (numerals). Lyons's preferred DP analysis treats articles as "typically free forms and pleonastic, lexically empty fillers" (1999: 321) contrasted with "substantive definite determiners, such as demonstratives" — i.e., the articles are the defective limit case even on his own account.

**Cross-linguistic context from Lyons.** Many languages lack articles entirely (1999: Ch 2); of those that have them, many use affixal articles (Scandinavian, Balkan), suggesting further phonological reduction of the type *the* shows. Demonstratives are universal; articles are not. Possessives, demonstratives, and personal pronouns consistently have more semantic content than articles (Lyons 1999: Ch 3). So cross-linguistically, *the/a*-type items are the *minimum end* of a grammaticalisation cline, not the typical centre of mass for determinatives.

**Det-as-noun redescription of the steelman.** On the superordinate-Noun view, *the* and *a* are the defective limit of the determinative sub-cluster — maximally grammaticalised, minimal descriptive content, locked to Det function. This is exactly what an HPC framework predicts: the sub-cluster has a profile (clustered determinative-noun properties), and *the/a* sit at the reduced end of the cluster where many properties (fused-head use, modifier use, phonological independence) have been lost through grammaticalisation. The fact that they never stand alone isn't a categorial problem for det-as-noun; it's a property of individual lexemes at the edge of their sub-cluster. Compare proper nouns: not all proper nouns take articles (*\*the John*), not all are pluralisable (*\*Johns*), and some have defective distribution (many place names reject determiners). Proper nouns are still nouns. Similarly *the/a* are still determinatives, and determinatives are still nouns.

**Strong/weak steelman.** **Empirically well-supported.** Lyons 1999 gives the full diachronic and typological ammunition: articles are derivative of demonstratives and numerals, phonologically weak, semantically minimal, cross-linguistically recent. PHP 2007: 22-23 note that defectiveness of *every* and *no* is already a lexical fact — so the move is well-precedented. The weakness is rhetorical: readers may feel *the/a* are the prototype determinatives (since they're the most frequent), and need to be convinced via the Lyons + HPC picture that they're actually the defective limit.

## Recommendation (≈100 words)

**Load-bearing payoff candidates: (i) fused-head and (iii) NP:Det uniformity, in that order.**

(i) is the strongest: eliminating fusion-of-functions (a substantial piece of 2007 theoretical machinery, explicitly defended by Payne et al. over ellipsis and null-head alternatives) is a real simplification with measurable analytical cost. (iii) is the cleanest formal consequence: Det function is uniformly NP-filled, not disjunctively "NP or DP." These two pair naturally — both concern the Det slot and its filler category — and together they eliminate the two places where CGEL's Ch 5 machinery creaks. (ii) is weaker because CGEL handles the cases already; (iv) risks creating a modifier-selection puzzle (adverbs on determinatives vs adjectives on common nouns) that needs separate handling.
