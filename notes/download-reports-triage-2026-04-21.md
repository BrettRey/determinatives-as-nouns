## Bottom line

Report 2 is useful on chronology if its labels are cleaned up. Report 1 is useful on source discovery and opposition structure if its uptake claims are toned down.

The main correction needed is simple: early Old English independent uses of demonstratives are not well described as `fused Det-Head` if the language has not yet developed a proper determiner function in the later sense. They are better treated as independent demonstrative/pronominal uses out of which later determiner uses develop.

## Report 2: Historical chronology

### Usable claims

1. Independent demonstrative/pronominal uses are very old.
   This is consistent with [CHEL I](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/Mendeley%20Desktop/Unknown/Unknown/Unknown%20-%201992%20-%20The%20Cambridge%20history%20of%20the%20English%20language%20Volume%20I%20The%20beginnings%20to%201066.pdf), which says Old English `se` and `þes` had both pronominal and adjectival/modifying functions.

2. Substantive adjectives are also very old.
   This is consistent with [CHEL I](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/Mendeley%20Desktop/Unknown/Unknown/Unknown%20-%201992%20-%20The%20Cambridge%20history%20of%20the%20English%20language%20Volume%20I%20The%20beginnings%20to%201066.pdf) and [CHEL II](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/Mendeley%20Desktop/Unknown/Unknown/Unknown%20-%201992%20-%20The%20Cambridge%20history%20of%20the%20English%20Language%20Volume%20II%201066%E2%80%931476.pdf): Old English and Early Middle English still have a broad system of adjectives used substantially.

3. The specifically modern `the poor / the rich` pattern is a narrowed residue.
   This aligns with [CHEL II](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/Mendeley%20Desktop/Unknown/Unknown/Unknown%20-%201992%20-%20The%20Cambridge%20history%20of%20the%20English%20Language%20Volume%20II%201066%E2%80%931476.pdf) and [CHEL III](/Users/brettreynolds/Documents/Mendeley%20Desktop/Unknown/Unknown/Unknown%20-%201999%20-%20The%20Cambridge%20history%20of%20the%20English%20Language%20Volume%20III%201476%E2%80%931776.pdf), both of which describe later English as restricting adjective heads rather than innovating them.

4. The rise of prop-word `one` belongs with the decline of the older substantive-adjective system.
   This is plausible and fits the Cambridge History line that adjective heads become restricted as the later system settles.

### Unsafe claims

1. Do not call independent Old English `se/þæt` a `strict fused determiner-head`.
   This imports a later analysis into a stage where the relevant dependent determiner function is still emergent. The report is strongest when it describes these as independent demonstrative/pronominal uses.

2. Do not treat the chronology as if there were a single race between `fused Mod-Head` and `fused Det-Head`.
   The older facts are not naturally described in those modern terms. The clean historical contrast is:
   - old independent demonstrative/pronominal uses
   - old substantive-adjective uses
   - later restriction of adjective heads
   - later stabilization of article/determiner systems

3. Do not let a silent-noun analysis of `the poor` leak backward into Old English.
   Whatever one thinks about later English, Old English adjective forms still carry the inflectional burden themselves, which makes a straightforward substantive-adjective reading much stronger there.

### Best use in the paper

- Use the report to support the claim that the modern residual `the poor` pattern should not be projected backward unchanged.
- Use it to support the idea that adjective-headed nominals are historically older and broader than the modern residue.
- Do not cite its “strict fused determiner-head” wording without rewriting it into historically neutral terms.

## Report 1: Subject-determiner uptake

### Usable claims

1. The exact `subject-determiner` label has had limited uptake outside CGEL-adjacent work.
   This fits the broader impression from the literature and from direct discussion already done in this project.

2. Payne is a real continuation point.
   That is worth keeping because it matters whether the category survives at all beyond CGEL itself.

3. The field more readily keeps the `determiner` part than the `subject` part.
   This is a good working hypothesis and seems directionally right.

4. Hudson- and Parrott-style alternatives matter because they replace the CGEL architecture, not merely its terminology.
   That is useful for §5 framing.

### Unsafe claims

1. The report overstates `uptake` by merging together several different things:
   - direct theoretical adoption
   - reuse of terminology
   - annotation uptake
   - historical citation
   - mere discussion as a foil

2. GREC and similar annotation or applied reuse should not be treated as evidence that the analysis became standard.
   They show portability of the label, not broad theoretical adoption.

3. The Aarts item should not be overweighted.
   It is useful as a direct expression of the objection, but it is not peer-reviewed and should not carry the burden of “the field rejected CGEL.”

4. The report’s positive/negative table should be treated as a source map, not as a settled adjudication.
   Several entries need direct checking before they can be used in prose.

### Best use in the paper

- Use the report to identify who must be discussed in the nearby-proposals section.
- Use it to sharpen the claim that the determiner part of CGEL’s analysis traveled better than the subject part.
- Do not use it to claim broad historical uptake of `subject-determiner` without direct source verification.

## Current paper implications

1. For the history section, the safest phrasing is:
   Old English already had independent demonstrative/pronominal heads and robust substantive adjectives; later English narrows the adjective-headed system and stabilizes a more determiner-centered one.

2. For the CGEL critique, the safer uptake claim is:
   `Subject-determiner` had selective continuation in CGEL-adjacent work, but little broad uptake as a general syntactic function label.

3. For the paper’s own argument, the best historical payoff is not “Det-Head arose before Mod-Head.”
   It is:
   - the adjective-headed pattern is old and once broad
   - the modern residual `the poor` pattern is late and narrow
   - early independent demonstratives should not be redescribed with later fused-head terminology

## One-sentence working summary

Use report 2 for chronology after stripping out its anachronistic fused-head labels for early demonstratives; use report 1 for source discovery after downgrading its uptake claims from adoption to selective continuation.
