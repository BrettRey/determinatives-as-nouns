# Phase 2A: Differentiation paragraphs for neighbour unification proposals

Four paragraphs distinguishing the "determinatives are a subtype of Noun" thesis from four existing proposals that unify pronoun/determinative/name. Each paragraph: what the neighbour claims, what the new paper shares with it, where the new paper diverges.

---

## Postal (1969 [1966])

Postal's claim is narrower than it is sometimes remembered to be: pronouns like *he, she, they, we, you* are "really articles, in fact types of definite article" - i.e., the direction of unification runs pronoun into determinative, not the reverse. The argument is transformational: so-called pronouns are segments added to NPs whose head nouns carry [+Pro, +Definite, ...], with the head noun typically deleted by a PRONOUN DELETION rule (leaving the would-be forms *Ione, heone, weones* as *I, he, we*). Evidence includes *we men, you guys, us Americans*, dialectal *we'uns/you'uns*, and the distribution of *one* as the underlying head. We share Postal's core empirical insight - that pronouns and determinatives pattern together nominally - and his suspicion of the traditional pronoun/determinative split. We diverge on direction and architecture: we do not derive pronouns transformationally from underlying [+Pro] nouns plus articles, and we do not reduce pronouns to articles; both sit as coordinate sub-kinds under a superordinate Noun.

## Hudson (2004)

Hudson argues that "determiners are a subset of pronouns" - specifically, the transitive pronouns, those whose valency allows a following common noun complement. His reasoning: since pronouns outnumber determiners and range over demonstrative, possessive, interrogative and other types, and since determiner-hood is just a valency property (taking a singular countable common noun, and only one such per noun), it is pointless to posit a separate word class *determiner*; the work can be done by treating *the, this, my* as pronouns that happen to be transitive. He then argues, on independent grounds, that N and D are mutually dependent, so either may head the NP. We share Hudson's conclusion that the determinative/pronoun boundary is not a categorial fault line and that *any/any spots*, *no/none*, *my/mine* are variants of the same items. We diverge by keeping determinative as a distinct sub-kind of Noun rather than collapsing it into pronoun, and by retaining the CGEL category/function distinction and N-as-head analysis that Hudson's Word Grammar architecture makes optional.

## Anderson (1997, manuscript)

Anderson's notionally grounded system, revived in his names manuscript (§3.1), groups names with pronouns and determiners as "all referential but non-predicable," representing them together as the functional category {N} (determination), distinct from the lexical category {N;P} (common noun, which is referential *and* predicable). The unifying property is primary identification: names, deictics and personal pronouns can identify an argument directly without descriptive content, unlike common nouns. Common noun thus sits outside the name/pronoun/determiner cluster in Anderson's taxonomy, which he sometimes labels the "determinatives." We share Anderson's intuition that name, pronoun and determinative cluster together behaviourally, and his reading of this as an HPC-friendly clustering of identifying properties. We diverge on where common noun belongs: for us it is a coordinate sub-kind under Noun, not a separate lexical category, and we do not adopt the {N}/{N;P} notional apparatus or the functional/lexical split that drives Anderson's architecture.

## Déchaine and Wiltschko (2002)

Déchaine and Wiltschko argue that "pronoun" is not a primitive but decomposes into three syntactic categories - pro-DP, pro-phiP, and pro-NP - distinguished by internal structure (full DP shell, bare phi-projection, bare NP), external distribution (argument only vs. argument-or-predicate vs. predicate), and binding behaviour (R-expression vs. variable vs. constant). English *one* is a pro-NP; 1st and 2nd person pronouns are pro-DPs; 3rd person pronouns are pro-phiPs. Their core move is fragmentation of the pronoun category into structurally distinct types to explain distributional asymmetries. We share their empirical observation that pronouns are not homogeneous and that sub-regularities crosscut the traditional class. We diverge architecturally and in direction: we keep pronoun as a single sub-kind of Noun rather than splitting it into three categories, and we reject the DP-shell analysis their typology presupposes ("the dog" remains an NP with DP in determiner function). Their internal heterogeneity lives, in our account, as within-subcluster variation, not as categorial fault lines.
