# Diagnostic case — for §3

Planning asset for §3 of the paper. The membership criterion is stated and tested in §3 (after §3.1's fused-head reanalysis). §2 supplies bridge evidence (paradigm split, genitive NPs in Det function); see `notes/paper-plan.md` §2.

## The membership criterion (stated at start of §3)

**A lexical category C is a sub-category of Noun iff items of C can head NPs (i.e., serve as Head N within NP structure) where those NPs fill subject, object, or PrepC functions, and where this Head function is part of the category's profile rather than reached via fusion of another function with Head.**

The criterion respects CGEL's phrasal architecture: lexical items head phrasal categories; phrasal categories serve syntactic functions. A lexical N heads an NP; the NP fills subject (etc.) function. Subject isn't directly a function of N.

**Profile level, not lexeme level.** Category membership is a property of the sub-category profile, not of every individual lexeme. A sub-category qualifies if items canonically head NPs as part of the sub-category's profile; lexically defective members are admitted on paradigmatic grounds. Compare proper nouns: most head NPs canonically (`\mention{Kim}`, `\mention{Toronto}`); `\mention{the Hague}` and similar place names are defective in requiring an article; all belong to the proper noun sub-category. The same allowance covers `\mention{the}` and `\mention{a}` within the determinative sub-category — defective members of a sub-category whose profile satisfies the criterion. §4 develops this.

**The non-fused condition** excludes items that head NPs only via the fusion-of-functions apparatus (Payne, Huddleston & Pullum 2007). Adjectives in `\mention{the rich}` head NPs via Mod-Head fusion. Adjectives don't head NPs as part of their lexical-category profile; they head AdjPs, which canonically fill modifier function within NP and predicative complement function within clauses. Mod-Head fusion is the exceptional case in which an AdjP is reanalysed as occupying NP Head function. Adjectives don't satisfy the criterion.

The Predet-Head residue mostly disappears under the proposal. CGEL items in Predeterminer function (`\mention{all, both, half}`) are also classified as determinatives in their non-Predet uses; their fused-head occurrences are simple fused-head, subsumed by §3.1. Predet-only items (`\mention{such, what a, many a, quite a, rather a}`) are structurally bound to a following Det+N sequence and cannot head NPs at all. Either way, no Predet-Head residue threatens the criterion.

Pronouns, common nouns, and proper nouns satisfy the criterion directly (CGEL Ch 5). Determinatives are the contested case. They appear as Head in fused-head uses (`\mention{some left}`). The membership question turns on whether fused-head use is a fusion construction (criterion not satisfied) or canonical headedness (criterion satisfied). §3.1 argues for the second reading using two positive discriminators.

## Bridge evidence (for §2; see `notes/paper-plan.md` §2)

§2 supplies two facts that make the §3 case more plausible without testing the criterion. They aren't repeated here in detail; see the plan. In summary:

- Stage A: the dependent possessives (CGEL-classified as determinatives) and the independent possessives (CGEL-classified as pronouns, hence as nouns) form a single inflectional paradigm split across two distinct primary categories.
- Stage B: genitive NPs (CGEL-analysed as NPs, headed by common or proper nouns) fill determiner function.

These show that the determiner function is already drawing on the noun system. They don't satisfy the membership criterion for determinatives — that's §3's work.

## Two positive discriminators for §3.1 (simple fused-head vs Mod-Head/Predet-Head fusion)

(a) **Paradigm productivity.** Fused-head use is a broadly productive pattern within the determinative class. The set that admits it includes `\mention{some, all, both, many, few, several, most, this/these, that/those, each, either, neither, much, enough}`. CGEL (371-2) and PHP (2007: 22-23) identify a small set of lexically defective members: `\mention{the, a}` lack fused-head use entirely, `\mention{every}` doesn't appear as a fused head, and `\mention{no}` is restricted to the suppletive `\mention{none}`. The defective set is small and independently noted; the pattern remains broadly productive across the rest of the class. Cardinals are out of scope per Reynolds 2026 and don't enter the discriminator. Adjective Mod-Head fusion is, by contrast, lexically restricted to a small set: `\mention{the rich, the poor, the elderly, the dead, the unemployed, the unobtainable}`, mostly adjectives describing classes of people. Broadly productive within determinatives; narrowly lexicalised within adjectives.

(b) **Structural saturation.** Simple fused-head NPs require no other element: `\mention{some left}`, `\mention{many came}`, `\mention{all agree}` are full NPs. Mod-Head fusion requires a determiner: `\mention{the rich}` is a full NP, but `\mention{*rich}` alone (as a class-referring NP) isn't. The Mod-Head AdjP can't saturate the NP on its own; the simple fused-head determinative can. Predet-only items (`\mention{such, what a}`) are even more bound — they require Det+N to follow and never head NPs at all.

These discriminators are independent of the categorial conclusion. They identify structural and distributional asymmetries that license different analyses across the three cases (simple fused-head, Mod-Head residue, Predet-only items).
