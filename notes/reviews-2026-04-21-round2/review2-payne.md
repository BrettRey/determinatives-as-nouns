# Review: "Determinatives as nouns" — John Payne (English Language and Linguistics)

## One-sentence summary

A two-stage argument that determinatives belong in the noun supercategory, in which the second stage proposes to dismantle most of the fusion-of-functions analysis I developed in Payne, Huddleston & Pullum (2007), but which engages my framework only obliquely and ducks the harder cases that motivated it in the first place.

## Strengths

1. **The two-stage architecture is a real improvement on a one-shot reanalysis.** Separating the partitive headhood claim (\textit{some of mine, all of those}) from the simple-fused-head reanalysis (\textit{Some left}) is methodologically sound. The partitives genuinely do show that a determinative can be the lexical pivot of a matrix NP that licenses an \mention{of}-PP, and that observation doesn't depend on resolving the fusion question. I can read §3.1 from the partitive paragraph through to the Mod-Head residue without flinching, which is more than I could say of earlier attempts in this tradition.

2. **The pronoun-parallel argument deserves serious engagement.** \textit{Mine is on the table} as a Nom-headed NP without a Det position is a fact about the existing CGEL analysis, and the parallel to \textit{Some left} is genuinely suggestive. The reasoning that a genitive-marked pronoun can head an NP without fusion (despite genitive marking elsewhere correlating with Det function) does at least raise a coherent question about why the same option shouldn't be available to determinatives.

3. **The internal-modification discriminator (\textit{the lucky few}) is a substantive empirical point** that the null-head and silent-NP literatures need to answer. The structural argument — that there's no syntactic position left for a silent head given where the determinative actually appears — is correctly stated and worth pressing.

## Weaknesses

1. **The paper doesn't actually engage Payne, Huddleston & Pullum (2007) on its own terms.** The 2007 paper sets out fusion of functions as a first-class theoretical construct with six defining properties (recoverability of the missing function, structural markedness of the fused realization, restrictions on the fusing categories, the relation to ellipsis, and so on). Reynolds cites the 2007 piece three times but never tabulates the six properties against the simple cases to show which ones the simple cases fail. The three discriminators (paradigm productivity, structural saturation, internal modification) are *new* tests built for this paper, not engagements with the diagnostic apparatus that motivated the fused-head analysis to begin with. The reader is told that fusion "drops out of most of its CGEL-Ch.~5 applications" without being shown that the simple cases lack the properties that originally pulled them in. This is the central technical gap.

2. **The productivity discriminator argues against the wrong target.** Reynolds compares the productivity of the simple cases (broadly productive across determinatives) with Mod-Head fusion in the AdjP cases (lexically restricted to \textit{the rich, the poor, the elderly}). But that asymmetry is well-known and was never the basis for treating the simple cases as fusion. Both PHP 2007 and CGEL Ch.~5 §9 distinguish the two patterns — the simple cases are productive *because* the determinative system is productive in Det function generally; the AdjP cases are restricted because adjectival reference to human classes is restricted. Showing that the two patterns differ in productivity tells us nothing about whether either is fusion. It tells us the patterns differ, which everyone already knew. A productivity asymmetry between two fusion sub-types doesn't refute either as fusion.

3. **The Mod-Head residue treatment is internally inconsistent with the discriminator logic.** §3.1 retains fusion for \textit{the rich, the poor} on the grounds that the AdjP can't saturate the NP (it needs \mention{the}). But the same saturation test applied to determinatives without an article (\textit{Some left} as opposed to *\textit{Rich left}) is what's said to *rule out* fusion for the simple cases. This makes the saturation diagnostic do diagnostic work in opposite directions for the two patterns: when the AdjP needs an article, that's evidence of fusion; when the determinative doesn't need one, that's also evidence against fusion. But unless the underlying claim is "fusion requires structural augmentation," neither inference goes through. And if that *is* the underlying claim, the paper needs to defend it explicitly. The 2007 framework does not require that fused realizations be structurally marked in the article-bearing sense Reynolds is using.

4. **The pronoun-parallel argument cuts the wrong way under CGEL's own assumptions.** Pronouns are nouns in CGEL, and Reynolds treats this as the relevant baseline. But the move he wants is precisely to extend nounhood to determinatives — so deriving determinative-headedness from a parallel to pronouns is circular at the supercategory level. Either pronouns are nouns *because* they head Noms in the relevant constructions (in which case the simple-fused-head reanalysis is what does the categorial work, not the parallel), or they're nouns on independent grounds (case inflection, paradigmatic profile) that determinatives don't share. The "exactly parallel" claim in the line about *Some left* having the same architecture as \textit{Mine is on the table} requires the conclusion (determinatives are nouns) as a premise.

5. **The partitive argument doesn't quite establish what the paper says it does.** Reynolds says partitives show "determinatives can head matrix NPs" independently of the fusion question. But the fusion analysis of \textit{some of the books} treats \mention{some} as the head-via-fusion of the matrix NP — this is exactly what fusion delivers. Saying "\mention{of} is licensed by the quantificational pivot" is consistent with both the canonical-headedness analysis and with the fused-head analysis (where the quantificational pivot is the determinative occupying the fused Det+Head position). The partitive evidence doesn't discriminate between the two analyses; it shows only that \mention{some} is structurally prominent enough to license \mention{of}-complementation, which both analyses already capture. The two-stage architecture is a real improvement *organizationally*, but the first stage doesn't carry the independent argumentative load the paper assigns to it.

## Key Q&A question

§3.1 reanalyses the simple fused-head cases as canonical head-of-NP without engaging the six diagnostic properties of fusion of functions in Payne, Huddleston & Pullum (2007). Could the author take three of those properties — say, recoverability of the missing function, the restriction to particular function pairs (Det+Head, Mod+Head, Predet+Head), and the parallel to ellipsis — and show explicitly which the simple cases fail? In particular: (a) Why is the recoverable Head content (anaphoric or generic) in \textit{I'll take some} not evidence for a fused Head function? (b) If the simple cases aren't fusion, why do they participate in the same Det+Head pairing that the productive system seems designed around, while not extending to combinations the fusion analysis correctly excludes? (c) On the proposed analysis, what's the principled reason for retaining fusion for Mod-Head while rejecting it for Det-Head — is the difference more than productivity-driven?

A second sub-question: the partitive argument claims to be independent of the fusion question, but in CGEL the partitive head \mention{some} in \textit{some of the books} is *itself* analysed under the fused-head apparatus. Could the author show that the partitive cases would be reanalysed as canonical headedness *without* presupposing the conclusion of stage two?

## Verdict

**Major revisions.** The paper has real value: the partitive observation, the pronoun-parallel as a research question, and the internal-modification cut against null heads all earn their keep. The two-stage architecture is the right structural move. But §3.1 doesn't engage the fusion-of-functions framework on its own diagnostic terms, the productivity argument targets a strawman, and the Mod-Head residue treatment uses the saturation diagnostic in inconsistent directions. Without an explicit confrontation with the six diagnostic properties from PHP 2007, the reader is asked to accept a substantial dismantling of the fused-head analysis on the strength of three new tests that were designed for the conclusion. I'd want to see (a) a property-by-property comparison with the 2007 apparatus, (b) a defence of the partitive argument against the charge of presupposing the conclusion, and (c) clarification of why the Mod-Head residue isn't subject to the same dissolution. With those additions, the paper could be a significant contribution. As it stands, it sidesteps the framework it most needs to address.

— John Payne
