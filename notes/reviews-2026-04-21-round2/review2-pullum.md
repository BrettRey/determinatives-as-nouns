# Review (Round 2): "Determinatives as nouns"

**Reviewer: Geoffrey K. Pullum**
**Journal: *English Language and Linguistics***

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper now argues for a four-sub-category Noun in two clearly separated stages: partitive constructions show that determinatives can head matrix NPs independently of any fusion claim, and the simple one-word cases (*Some left*, *I'll take some*) are reanalysed as ordinary head-of-NP function on three positive discriminators — paradigm productivity, structural saturation, and internal modification — yielding a single phrase type at the noun-headed determiner slot and a defective-member account of *the* and *a*.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The two-stage separation is the right move and is now executed cleanly.** Splitting partitive headhood off from the fusion question, before the simple cases are touched, is the architectural improvement the previous draft most needed. The partitive argument in §3 is now genuinely independent: *some of mine*, *many of them*, *all of those* are matrix NPs whose pivot is the determinative, the *of*-PP is licensed by the quantificational pivot exactly as in *some of the books*, and a covert head adds no independent evidence. That isolates a result the rest of the paper can lean on without circularity. Even a reader who refuses the §3.1 reanalysis is now committed to the limited but important point that determinatives can head matrix NPs at all. That is the right place to plant the flag.

- **The §3.2 simplification claim is now sharpened in a way I find difficult to resist.** The new presentation is no longer a notational tidy-up. The author identifies a specific descriptive stipulation in CGEL — that the head N selects "DP or NP (genitive)" with the parallelism (definiteness contribution, one-Det-per-NP, fused-head licensing) accommodated as a coincidence between two phrase types — and argues that under the proposal the parallelism is a consequence of both being NPs. That is a genuine theoretical gain over the disjunctive analysis, and it is stated with the appropriate restraint: the simplification is at the phrase-type level, not the feature level, and PPs in Det function are explicitly preserved alongside. This is the kind of argument I asked for in the previous round (the head-governs-selection worry), and it does land.

- **The Mod-Head residue is now carved out with the right empirical profile.** The paper now explicitly says (§3.1) that fusion remains motivated for the *the rich* set on the narrow profile of lexical restriction and required determiner, and the table (Table 1) makes the contrast graphic. The earlier draft's worry — that the three discriminators might equally tell us the Mod-Head cases are conversion rather than fusion — is reduced by the two new structural facts: (i) productivity contrast (broadly productive vs narrowly lexicalized), and (ii) saturation contrast (*Some left* vs *\*Rich get richer*). Reasonable people can still take the Bruening-style line, but the paper now makes the asymmetric treatment defensible rather than merely asserted.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The defective-member account of *the* and *a* in §4 still rests largely on paradigmatic-contrast reasoning, and the addition of *every* makes the inferential structure more fragile, not less.** I asked in the previous round for at least one positive diagnostic that *the* and *a* pass and common-noun Det-fillers (e.g. *Kim's*) fail. §4 still does not supply one. The three considerations it offers — paradigmatic opposition, featural profile, and confinement to Det function — are all consistent with *the* and *a* being members of a class whose distributional behaviour they fail to share, not evidence that they are nouns. The grammaticalization story is illuminating but explicitly described as not doing the synchronic membership work. Worse, the new paragraph on *every* extends the same reasoning by lumping it with *the*, *a*, *no* as "a small set of determinatives with distributional attrition at the fused-head end." That is fine if the determinative-as-noun claim is established for the rest of the class, but it now means the case for the sub-category's nounhood rests almost entirely on the productive members — *some*, *all*, *both*, *many*, *few*, the demonstratives, and so on — with at least four members admitted as defective in exactly the diagnostic that §3.1 introduces. The paper needs to confront the question whether four defective members in a sub-category of (say) twenty is paradigm defectiveness or a substantial categorial split. In the auxiliary-verb analogue (Pullum & Wilson 1977), the defective members (*must*, *ought*) are defective in agreement with verbal morphology, not in the very property used to argue they are verbs. The articles' defectiveness is structurally different and the paper does not adequately address that asymmetry. Actionable: tighten the comparison with auxiliary defectiveness, and either supply the requested positive diagnostic for the articles or concede that they sit on the boundary in a way the demonstratives and quantifiers do not.

- **The internal-modification argument in §3.1 is good but the structural reasoning at lines 119–120 is harder to follow than it needs to be, and one alternative is not properly ruled out.** The argument is that AdjP modifiers in *the lucky few* attach to *few* as the lexical pivot, ruling out null-head and silent-NP analyses because there is no syntactic position left for a silent head given where the determinative appears. The prose runs the linear-order argument and the structural argument together, and the parenthetical (*the lucky __ few* is not the structure) is doing the work of an example without being marked as one. More substantively, a defender of the silent-NP analysis can answer: the silent NP is the immediate complement of *few*, with the AdjP attached at NP level; the determinative does not need to be in pre-modifier position because it is at a higher functional layer. That move is unattractive on independent grounds (it reintroduces the DP architecture the paper rejects), but the §3.1 argument should make the rejection explicit rather than relying on the reader to supply it. The point can be made cleanly in two sentences. Actionable: rewrite lines 119–120 to separate the linear-position argument from the structural-position argument and explicitly note that the silent-head defender has to embrace a DP-type architecture the paper rejects.

- **The bridge section (§2) is now narrower in scope than it should be, and one of its old strengths has been quietly dropped.** The previous draft made the dependent-vs-independent possessive paradigm (*my*/*mine*) split do work as motivation: the same paradigm is split across two primary categories under the existing CGEL analysis, and that is a genuine cost. The current §2 makes the weaker claim that the determiner function is already filled by NPs headed by nouns under existing CGEL analysis. That is true and useful, but it is not a cost of the existing taxonomy; it is just a fact CGEL already accepts. The bridge accordingly does less softening of the ground than it could. I am not asking for the previous draft's framing back wholesale (the new section is more focused), but the paper would benefit from at least one sentence noting that the *my*/*mine* paradigm split across primary categories is itself a motivation for the proposal, even if the load-bearing argument is in §3.

---

## 4. Key question at Q&A

You now have a clean two-stage argument: partitives establish determinative headhood, and three discriminators reanalyse the simple cases as canonical head function. The articles are admitted as defective members. But four members of the determinative class — *the*, *a*, *every*, and *no* (apart from suppletive *none*) — are defective in exactly the diagnostic that §3.1 uses to license the determinative-as-noun claim, and *the* and *a* are defective in *every* distributional test the criterion applies. In the auxiliary-verb analogue you cite, *must* and *ought* are defective in tense/agreement, not in the property that argues they are verbs. What is the principled basis for treating defectiveness in the very diagnostic property as paradigm defectiveness rather than a categorial split — and at what proportion of defective members would you concede the sub-category has fractured rather than degraded at one end?

---

## 5. Verdict

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The two-stage argument now holds together, the §3.2 simplification claim earns its keep, and the residual fusion analysis for Mod-Head is no longer doing rhetorical work it cannot bear. The remaining issues are real but local: the defective-member account of the articles needs the positive-diagnostic question addressed (or conceded), the internal-modification argument needs a small structural rewrite, and the bridge section deserves one sentence of restoration on the *my*/*mine* paradigm split. None of these requires restructuring. The paper is now closer to publishable than to revisable, and an editor would be within their rights to accept on revisions of this scope.

## CGEL-fidelity check

I checked the paper's characterizations of CGEL claims as I read. The following are correctly attributed:

- Determiner function admits DPs, genitive NPs, and PPs (CGEL Ch. 5 §4–5; cited at line 160).
- Fusion of functions for *Some left* / *I'll take some* (CGEL Ch. 5 §9; cited at line 73).
- Pronouns as a sub-category of noun (CGEL ch. 5; the paper relies on this without separate citation, which is fine).
- Lexically defective fused-head members (*the*, *a*, *every*, *no*/*none*) (CGEL pp. 371–2; cited at line 96 and 188; and Payne 2007 pp. 22–23).
- The *the rich* / *the poor* set as Mod-Head fusion (CGEL Ch. 5 §9; correctly described at lines 92, 96, 105–110).

One characterization I'd push back on lightly: at line 92 the paper says "Mod-Head fusion, exemplified by AdjPs in *the rich*, *the poor*, *the elderly*, is a smaller residue." CGEL's own discussion treats Mod-Head fusion as a distinct sub-type of fused-head construction with its own properties, not as the residue left after simple fused-head; the paper's framing is an editorial move motivated by its own argument, which is fair, but a footnote acknowledging the relabelling would be appropriate.

## Source-grounding flags

I have not re-verified bibliographic data during this round; I assume the previous round's `/validate-bib` recommendation has been carried out. I flag two specific items the author should double-check before resubmission:

- **Spinillo 2000 / 2004**: the paper now characterizes Spinillo as proposing "a positive repartitioning in which demonstratives and genitives join pronouns, quantifiers split across classes, and *the*, *a*, and *every* form a residual article class" (line 234). This matches my recollection but I have not opened Spinillo to confirm; the author should check the page reference for the *every*-in-articles claim specifically.

- **Heine & Kuteva 2002 lexicon**: the cross-linguistic paths DEMONSTRATIVE > DEFINITE ARTICLE and NUMERAL ONE > INDEFINITE ARTICLE are correctly attributed in spirit, but the citation should specify pages or entry headings rather than the volume as a whole.

Beyond these, the citation pattern looks consistent with the previous round.
