# Review of "Determinatives as nouns" for *English Language and Linguistics*

**Reviewer:** Richard Hudson (UCL, retired)
**Recommendation:** Major revisions
**Date:** 2026-04-20

---

## Summary of the paper

The author argues that English has four coordinate sub-categories of a supercategory **Noun**: common noun, proper noun, pronoun, and determinative. The argument has two stages. First, partitive constructions (*some of mine*, *all of those*) show that determinatives head matrix NPs. Second, the simple cases that *CGEL* (Huddleston & Pullum 2002) treats as fused Det-Head (*Some left*, *I'll take some*) are reanalysed as ordinary head-of-NP function, on three positive discriminators (paradigm productivity, structural saturation, internal modification). The articles *the* and *a* are handled as defective members of a sub-category whose profile satisfies the criterion. Two simplifications follow: the DP/genitive-NP disjunction at Det collapses to NP, and the fused-head apparatus drops out of most of its *CGEL* applications.

I'll take the standard format: things to praise (Rapoport's first three), then the substantive worries.

## What the paper gets right

I want to begin by acknowledging real strengths, several of them non-obvious.

1. **The partitive headhood argument is well posed.** The author rightly separates the headhood question from the fusion question (§3, lines 76--86). Showing that *some of mine* requires *some* to be the matrix pivot before any fusion question is settled is exactly the right structure for the dialectic. Many writers conflate the two.

2. **The three diagnostics for the simple cases are well chosen.** Productivity, saturation, and internal modification (§3.1) are the right kind of test, and the comparison with the Mod-Head residue (*the rich*) is fair and informative. The internal-modification argument from *the lucky few* is particularly nice; I take it the author has read Payne et al. carefully and noticed where the fusion analysis cannot quite reach.

3. **The treatment of *every* in §4 is honest.** The author resists the temptation, taken by Spinillo (2004), of grouping *every* with *the* and *a* as a residual article class. That's the right call on distributional grounds, and I learned something from the framing of attrition-at-the-fused-head end as a separate dimension from attrition-at-non-Det-functions.

4. **The articles section is the most courageous part of the paper.** Defectiveness arguments are always vulnerable to the charge of saving the theory, but the author confronts the worst case directly and supplies three independent grounds (paradigmatic opposition, featural content, distributional cline) plus a grammaticalisation account. I don't agree with the conclusion, but the argument is well structured.

5. **The point that the DP/genitive-NP disjunction in *CGEL* is a stipulation that the new account dissolves (§3.2)** is genuinely a reduction in description, not just a rebranding. I note this because reductions of *that* kind are rare in syntactic taxonomy and worth flagging when they happen.

## Substantive concerns

Now the harder part. Four issues, in declining order of seriousness.

### 1. The mutual-dependency argument for D-N headedness is cited but not engaged

This is the central problem with the paper. My 2004 paper appears in the comparison table (§5, line 221) and in a single sentence at line 238: "[Postal, Hudson, Anderson] unify the same territory in the opposite direction or under a broader notional cover term. Their shared point is that the determiner-pronoun boundary isn't a clean categorial divide. The present paper agrees with that much. It diverges by refusing to reduce one noun-like sub-category to another or to leave common nouns outside the same supercategory."

That isn't engagement; that's noting that we disagree. The 2004 paper makes a specific structural argument that isn't addressed: D and N are *mutually* dependent, and which of them heads the NP in a given construction is determined by the construction. The evidence isn't a stipulation, it's a convergence of:

- **NP adjuncts** (*I saw him this morning*, Hudson 2004:11--14): only N is relevant to whether the NP can be used as an adjunct. *This morning* is fine; *the morning* and *a morning* are not. The lexical restrictions on adjunct-eligible nouns (way, time, day, *not* manner, point, party) survive even into relative clauses (*the way he did it*). On any analysis where D heads the NP, this lexical sensitivity to N has to be smuggled in. On a mutual-dependency analysis, it falls out.
- **Possessive parallels in Dutch and German** (Hudson 2004:14--15): *moeders jurk* vs. *Karls Freund* are simply genitive nouns in determiner function, with no \\\\_'s required. The English *the old man's hat* / *the hat of the old man* synonymy points the same way.
- **Fusion with prepositions** (French *du* = *de + le*; Hudson 2004:22--23, *for each = per*): the "preposition-fuses-with-D" pattern requires D to depend on the preposition, not on N. Author dismisses this kind of cross-linguistic point in §4 (line 204) as outside scope, but the dialectic with my position isn't avoidable that way: I argue from these patterns to a conclusion about the *English* NP architecture.
- **Anaphoric ellipsis** (Hudson 2004:25--29): the elliptical N after D (*Mary's bike is cleaner than John's*) patterns with anaphoric ellipsis after a verb or preposition (which is confined to *complements*), not with head-noun ellipsis after adjectives (which is a different beast: requires contrasting modifier, no cataphora, semantically restricted modifiers). If D's complement is N, the ellipsis is covered by the general rule for complement ellipsis. If N heads everything, you need a separate rule for the determinative cases.

The author's framework is phrase-structural, and Word Grammar is dependency-based, so I don't expect the argument to be transposed wholesale. But the *facts* the argument rests on (adjunct eligibility, complement ellipsis pattern) are framework-neutral. A CGEL-N-head-only analysis owes us an account of why N-properties leak out to determine NP-external distribution, while a determinative-headed analysis would have a story (the determinative inherits NP-external distribution from N as its dependent). The mutual-dependency proposal sits in between, and the present paper needs to choose against it on something other than terminological inheritance.

This connects to the second worry.

### 2. The argument from "fewer sub-categories is better" cuts the other way

The author proposes four sub-categories of Noun: common, proper, pronoun, determinative. I propose three: common, proper, pronoun (with determinatives as a sub-set of pronouns, the "transitive" ones). All else being equal, three is fewer than four. The paper owes a positive argument for the extra sub-category.

The §6 sub-cluster differences won't bear that weight. Claiming that "determinatives differ in modifier selection, semantic reduction, and restriction to Det and Mod functions" (line 265) is true but compatible with both partitions: on my account, transitive pronouns differ from intransitive ones in exactly the same ways. The Reynolds (2021) energy-distance result (line 261) shows determinatives and pronouns are distributionally distinct at 93%. Fine — but distinct *sub-clusters* within a unified pronoun class is consistent with that result; Reynolds (2021) doesn't independently motivate two coordinate sub-categories rather than a sub-clustered pronoun class.

The honest reading of the paper's own data is: there are three nominal sub-clusters whose distributions are sufficiently distinct to be statistically separable, and one residual question is whether the closed-class subcluster has internal structure (intransitive pronouns vs transitive ones) or external structure (pronouns vs determinatives). The author opts for the second; I opt for the first. The paper doesn't show why the second is superior.

I note in passing that the "transitive pronoun" reduction also explains why some pronouns alternate freely with a noun complement (*all*, *some*, *each*, *both*, *most*, *many*, *few*, *several*, *enough*, *this/that*, *some*) while others don't (*the, a, every, no*). The defective members on my account are a familiar valency phenomenon: *try* takes either an NP or a clause complement; *attempt* takes only an NP; *into* takes an NP, *in* alternates. Defective valency is everywhere in the lexicon and needs no separate apparatus. On the four-sub-category account, the defectiveness is an exception to the sub-category profile and needs the special pleading of §4 to absorb it.

### 3. The category/function distinction is doing less work than the paper claims

The author leans heavily on the CGEL category/function distinction throughout, and treats my framework's dispensing with it (Hudson 2000) as a cost. I'd push back here, gently. The category/function distinction does real work in some places (Pred*ic*ator vs. Pred*icate* in clauses; Subject vs. NP). Its work in the present paper, though, is largely rhetorical. Consider:

- The author argues *some* heads an NP in *Some left* (§3.1). On Word Grammar, *some* heads its own structure (a small NP) and depends on *left*, with the same surface result. We don't need to say that *some* is "in Head function of an NP" — we just say it's the head.
- The DP/NP collapse argument in §3.2 is real progress, but its punch-line is that what was a categorial disjunction in *CGEL* (DP or genitive NP) becomes a single NP type. On a framework with no D/N category split in the first place, that disjunction never arose. The author's reform recovers, in CGEL terms, what dependency-based frameworks already had.
- The fused-head residue (Mod-Head) is preserved (§3.1 end), but with the simple cases gone, it's a small lexicalised set. On my view, those cases (*the rich*) involve a determiner taking an adjective directly as its dependent, which is admittedly unusual but doesn't require a separate fusion construct. Reduction is reduction.

This isn't a knockdown — frameworks differ in what counts as a virtue — but the author should be more candid that the four-sub-category move *plus* the category/function machinery delivers, in CGEL terms, what dependency frameworks already deliver more directly. The paper presents the category/function distinction as a benefit that distinguishes it from rival approaches; in fact, half of its work is just translating between formalisms.

### 4. The articles aren't quite the worst case — *the* in correlatives is

§4 (line 196) acknowledges that *the* appears outside Det in the correlative comparative (*the bigger the better*) but says "its analysis is contested." That's too quick. The classic analysis (going back at least to Jespersen) has *the* here as a degree adverbial, distributionally adverbial not nominal. If that's right, *the* has an extra-nominal life that the defectiveness story doesn't predict. If *the* is a defective noun whose profile is "Det function only," the correlative *the* is a problem; if the correlative *the* is a homonym, that needs to be argued. Either way, dismissing it in a parenthetical doesn't suffice.

Relatedly: the author's line that the articles "carry exactly the features the determinative sub-category's profile predicts" (§4, line 194) glosses over the fact that *the* and *a* contribute *only* those features. Contrast with the demonstratives, which contribute deixis as well. On a sub-category where the prototypical members have rich content, the articles are at the lexical pole, not just the distributional pole. That's compatible with the author's account but worth noting; it's also compatible with my account, on which *the* is a thin pronoun whose lexical content has eroded to definiteness alone.

## Smaller points

- §2 (line 64): "the determiner function then admits noun-headed NPs from all four sub-categories of \\\\term{noun}, in addition to the non-noun-headed phrases CGEL already accepts in Det function (PPs like *about twenty*)." This is awkward: PPs in Det are extremely marginal and often analysed differently. The paper's central simplification claim (§3.2) shouldn't depend on accepting them as straightforwardly Det-functioning.

- §3.1 (line 92): "(Items like *all*, *both*, *half* fill predeterminer-modifier function in some constructions and are determinatives elsewhere; they fall under the simple-fused-head treatment below.)" But on my analysis, *all the dogs* is naturally analysed with *all* as a separate determinative depending on *the dogs*; the "predeterminer-modifier" function is parochial CGEL machinery. The paper deserves a fuller engagement with these cases than a parenthetical.

- §3.1 discriminator 3 (lines 113--120): the argument against null-head and silent-NP alternatives is ingenious, but the parallel to *the lucky survivors* presupposes that the AdjP attachment site is "immediately before the lexical pivot." On a non-configurational analysis (mine), there's no positional argument because there's no fixed structural template. The discriminator works against generative alternatives but not against dependency ones.

- §6 (line 263): the cardinal-numerals discussion claims that the present account simplifies Reynolds (2026 EL&L) by recasting cross-categorial alternation as within-category alternation. But that's only true if all four nominal sub-categories are coordinate. On a transitive-pronoun analysis, cardinals are pronouns (with various valency profiles); on a Hudson 1990 view, cardinals are nouns proper. Either way, the simplification is a function of the supercategory commitment, not an independent argument for it.

- The bibliography has Hudson 2004 and Hudson 1990 cited but not Hudson 2000 or 2010. Hudson 2000 ("Grammar without functional categories") is more directly relevant to the present paper's project than Hudson 2004, because it argues that "determiner" isn't a primary lexical category at all — exactly the negative claim the paper is also making. The author should cite it and engage with it.

- Spinillo's reductive move (cited at lines 234) deserves more space than it gets. If the author rejects Spinillo's repartition, the basis should be argued, not just stated. ("Demonstratives stay with determinatives rather than moving to pronouns" — why? Spinillo had reasons.)

## What the paper needs

A revision that does three things:

1. **Engage Hudson 2004's mutual-dependency argument substantively.** Not just the citation in Table 2, but a section (or substantial part of §5) that takes the adjunct, possessive, and ellipsis evidence on its own terms and says either (a) the four-sub-category proposal accounts for it, (b) the evidence is wrong, or (c) the evidence is irrelevant to the present argument and why. The current treatment is a mention, not an engagement.

2. **Add a positive argument for four sub-categories rather than three.** The paper needs to show why pronoun and determinative are coordinate sub-categories of Noun rather than determinative being a sub-class of pronoun. The Reynolds (2021) result is a starting point but not a conclusion.

3. **Be more candid about what the category/function distinction adds.** The §3.2 collapse is the cleanest illustration of the framework's payoff. The other places where the distinction is invoked (saturation, productivity) don't depend on it, and the rhetorical contrast with dependency frameworks would land better if the author distinguished where the architecture earns its keep from where it's working as translation infrastructure.

If those three things are done, this is a publishable paper that contributes to a long-running debate. As it stands, it argues a position without sufficiently engaging the closest opposing position, which is both a fairness issue and a strategic mistake — the sceptical reader (this one) is left wondering what the answer would be.
