# Review of "Determinatives as nouns"

**Reviewer:** Christopher Lyons (signed)
**For:** *English Language and Linguistics*
**Date:** 2026-04-20

---

## Summary of the submission

The paper argues that English determinatives form a fourth sub-category of \term{noun}, alongside common nouns, proper nouns, and pronouns. The architecture is explicitly non-Abneyan: the category/function distinction is preserved, and "the dog" remains an NP with a DP-now-NP in determiner function. The central two-stage argument is that (i) partitives like *some of mine* establish determinatives as heads of matrix NPs, and (ii) simple "fused-head" cases like *Some left* are reanalysed as ordinary head-of-NP function rather than as fusion of Det+Head. The articles *the* and *a* are treated as defective members of a sub-category whose profile satisfies the criterion. The scope is restricted to synchronic English. Section 4 (the articles) is the most directly relevant portion for me as reviewer.

---

## What I learned, and what I want to credit

I should say at the outset that this is a careful and unusually well-targeted piece of work. The author has done what most CGEL-internal proposals fail to do: explicitly separate the headhood question from the fusion question. The partitive evidence in §3 is well-chosen and the three discriminators (productivity, saturation, internal modification) are genuinely positive diagnostics rather than negative arguments by elimination. I learned something from the *the lucky few* argument against the silent-head analysis: the linear-position objection is sharper than any version of the same argument I've encountered.

I also want to credit the author for handling *the* and *a* as a problem rather than as decoration. Most proposals in this neighbourhood either pretend the articles fit the pattern or quietly retreat to "well, those are special." The defective-member move, with the appeal to my Chapter 9 to motivate why the defectiveness is exactly what it is, is the right shape of argument. Whether it works is another question, which I take up below.

The treatment of the genitive parallel in §2 is good — particularly the observation that the DP/genitive-NP disjunction in CGEL's Det position is accommodated rather than predicted, and that collapsing it to NP turns a coincidence into a structural consequence. This is a real gain.

---

## Major issues

### 1. The English-only restriction is the central weakness

I will be direct: the synchronic-English scope is a problem, and I do not think the author has adequately defended it. The paper invokes my Chapter 9 to motivate the defective status of *the* and *a* — that is, it leans on cross-linguistic and diachronic evidence — but then declines to do the cross-linguistic work that would test whether the four-noun architecture survives outside English.

This is not a request for a typological survey appended to a synchronic paper. It is a question about whether the argument is principled. If determinatives are nouns in English because they head NPs, satisfy the membership criterion, etc., then either:

(a) the same criterion applied to other languages will yield analogous results (and the author should show that it does, in at least a handful of cases), or

(b) the criterion is genuinely English-specific because of distributional facts that don't generalise (and the author should say what those are).

The current text gestures at (a) without committing, and doesn't engage seriously with (b). The closing remarks of §4 mention Scandinavian suffixal articles, Bulgarian/Romanian postposed definiteness, and article-less languages, and then defer all of them to "a question for separate work." But the typology I survey in *Definiteness* (1999, Ch. 9.1.3) sets up three language types — Type I (no DP), Type II (pronominal DP only), Type III (full DP) — and the four-noun proposal will play very differently in each. In Type I languages (Japanese, Korean, Dyirbal in my classification), there are no articles at all; the demonstrative is the most articulated nominal determiner. Are demonstratives nouns there too, on the proposal? Presumably yes, since they are presumably the closed-class counterpart to the open-class N. But then the "defective member" move can't do the work it does for English, because the entire defective end of the cline is missing.

The author needs to say one of two things:

(i) The four-noun architecture is a substantive synchronic claim about English that may or may not generalise, and the cross-linguistic question is genuinely open. Fine — but then the appeal to grammaticalization in §4 cannot be doing load-bearing work, because grammaticalization is by its nature a cross-linguistic story, and you don't get to invoke it selectively.

(ii) The four-noun architecture is a candidate cross-linguistic generalization, with English as the test case. Fine — but then at minimum, the author needs to show that the criterion picks out a coherent class in two or three other well-described languages, and to flag the cases where it doesn't.

The current text is neither. It uses cross-linguistic typology as scenery for the English argument while explicitly disclaiming any cross-linguistic commitment. That is the move I find evasive.

### 2. The grammaticalization story in §4 is correct in outline but underspecified at the joints

The claims about article diachrony are essentially right but stated more loosely than the source warrants. Let me go through them:

**(a) "*The* descends from the Old English distal demonstrative *þæt/se/sēo*."**

This conflates the paradigm with the singular ancestor. The actual diachrony is that ME *the* descends primarily from the OE form *se* (masc.nom.sg), with the *þ-* generalised analogically from the oblique stems (*þone*, *þæs*, *þam*, etc.). The neuter *þæt* survives separately as Modern English *that*. The feminine *sēo* doesn't survive into Modern English at all. So the gloss "descends from the OE distal demonstrative *þæt/se/sēo*" is not quite right; it would be more accurate to say "descends from the OE demonstrative paradigm *se/sēo/þæt*" (note: the standard order lists masculine first), with *the* coming primarily through the *se* form and the *þ-* being analogically extended.

I would also push back a little on "distal." The OE paradigm *se/sēo/þæt* is the generic or default demonstrative, contrasting with proximal *þes/þēos/þis* (which gives Modern *this*). Calling it "distal" is acceptable in a loose sense (it became Modern *that* in the neuter) but at the OE stage it's better described as the unmarked demonstrative. This is the form that, on my Chapter 9 account, is the natural source for definite articles precisely because it has the least deictic content to lose.

**(b) "*A/an* descends from the Old English numeral *ān* 'one'."**

This is correct, and the citation to Heine & Kuteva is appropriate. I would note that the author's own paper on numerals (Reynolds 2026) makes the case for *one* having complex distributional behaviour, and a footnote drawing the connection — the indefinite article descends from a numeral that, on the author's own analysis, sits on a spectrum the rest of the determinatives don't quite span — would strengthen rather than weaken the main argument.

**(c) The citation to Heine & Kuteva 2002 for the cross-linguistic paths.**

DEMONSTRATIVE > DEFINITE ARTICLE and NUMERAL "ONE" > INDEFINITE ARTICLE are indeed in their lexicon and are well-attested. No quarrel with the substance. But I will flag for the author that I do not have access to verify the H&K citation directly in the materials provided to me, and at least one paper I've reviewed recently has had H&K page numbers fabricated. Worth double-checking the page reference if the author hasn't already.

**(d) Citation to my own work.**

The citation "[Lyons] Ch. 9 surveys the cross-linguistic picture" is accurate and the use is appropriate. I appreciate that the author actually read the chapter rather than citing it as scenery. However, my Ch. 9 makes a stronger and more specific claim than is mobilised here: the *direction* of grammaticalization (DEM > ART, NUM > ART) is robust, but the *resulting* synchronic category is highly variable. Articles can be free forms, suffixes, prefixes, clitics, infixes, or absent entirely (Type I). Article-like elements can derive from verbs ("see"), classifiers, deictic particles, genitive morphemes — not just demonstratives and numerals. The author is right that the DEM and NUM paths are the most attested, but the universe of possible source categories is much broader than the paper acknowledges. This matters because the "noun source > noun endpoint" continuity that is implicit in the paper's argument (the articles' sources, demonstratives and numerals, are nominal on the present analysis) is a contingent feature of the English case, not a typological universal.

### 3. The "defective members of a sub-category whose profile satisfies the criterion" argument: does it work for *the* and *a*?

This is the load-bearing move and it deserves serious scrutiny. I am sympathetic but not yet convinced.

The author identifies three considerations:

(i) Paradigmatic opposition with demonstratives and quantifiers in feature space.
(ii) The articles carry exactly the features the sub-category profile predicts.
(iii) The articles are nearly confined to Det function, while other determinatives alternate productively into Mod and Head.

Take these in turn.

**On (i):** The paradigmatic opposition is real. *The* contrasts with *this/that* and with *some/any/every* in featural terms. But "paradigmatic opposition with X" is a much weaker condition than "shares the categorial profile of X." Across a large range of languages, articles stand in paradigmatic opposition with demonstratives and quantifiers without forming a single category with them — German *der* contrasts with *dieser* and *jeder* but also has distinctive inflectional and distributional properties that differentiate it. The paradigmatic-opposition argument needs to be strengthened by showing that the opposition is the kind of opposition that holds within a sub-category, not the kind that holds across sub-categories. The paper does not do this work.

**On (ii):** This is question-begging as stated. "The articles carry the features the sub-category's profile predicts" assumes that the sub-category's profile is the relevant thing they carry. The features (definiteness, count/non-count selection, number specification) are a smaller set than the full sub-category profile (which also includes Mod and Head distribution, modifier compatibility, etc.). The articles carry the *Det-relevant* features of the sub-category and lack the *Head-relevant* features. Whether that counts as "the profile the sub-category predicts" depends on which subset of the profile you take to be definitional.

**On (iii):** Yes, but this is exactly the property that has been used for over half a century to argue that articles are *not* in the same category as demonstratives and quantifiers. The author treats it as evidence of "defectiveness within the sub-category"; the more standard treatment (and mine) is that it's evidence of grammaticalization to a distinct functional category. The grammaticalization story in §4 is consistent with both readings, but the paper presents it as if it supports only the first. It does not.

I think the author needs a fourth consideration to make this stick: something positive about the synchronic behaviour of *the* and *a* that distinguishes them from elements that have grammaticalized out of the source category. The correlative comparative (*the bigger the better*) is mentioned and dismissed as "marginal" and "contested." That dismissal is too quick. If *the* in correlative comparatives is a genuinely deictic remnant — and on at least some analyses it is — then this is a non-defective use of *the* outside Det function, and it would support the sub-category-membership claim much better than anything else the section offers. I'd encourage the author to say more here, or to find a different positive consideration.

### 4. The treatment of *every*: fine, but the argument is thin

The author argues that *every* belongs with the quantifiers, contra Spinillo, on three grounds: it contributes universal quantification, selects singular count heads like *each*, and admits AdvP modifiers (*almost every, nearly every*).

The semantic and combinatorial arguments are reasonable. But the AdvP-modifier argument is the strongest one and the author doesn't develop it. *Almost every* and *nearly every* pattern with *almost all*, *nearly all*, *almost no*, *almost any* — a class of universal/negative quantifier modifications that *the* and *a* simply don't admit (*\*almost the, \*nearly a*). This is a clean distributional contrast and it does support placing *every* with the quantifiers.

The weakness in the section is the disposal of Spinillo. The author says Spinillo's grouping of *every* with the articles is on "semantic and distributional grounds" but doesn't engage with what Spinillo's grounds actually are. Spinillo (2000, 2004) — and I should say I find her framework congenial in many respects — argues for the grouping on the basis of certain distributional restrictions that are shared by *the*, *a*, and *every* but not by the demonstratives or other quantifiers (notably, the absence of fused-head use). The author's response is essentially "lexical defectiveness for fused-head use doesn't pull *every* out of the quantifier sub-group any more than it pulls *the/a* out of the determinatives as a whole." That's a fair point, but it relies on the author's own analysis being granted. From within Spinillo's analysis, the response is question-begging.

The right move, I think, is to acknowledge that on Spinillo's framework, the grouping makes sense, and then to show that on the four-noun framework (which the paper is arguing for), the *every*-with-quantifiers grouping is more natural because the universal quantifier features are more substantive than the article features. The paper essentially does this, but it needs to be more explicit that the disagreement with Spinillo is downstream of the larger architectural disagreement.

### 5. The claim that *the* and *a* are "the worst case" understates the difficulty

In §4 the author writes that *the* and *a* are "the worst case" for the proposal, then handles them in three pages. This is too quick for what they are: not the worst case but the *most diagnostic* case. The articles are where grammaticalization has gone furthest, where the categorial profile is most attenuated, and where the cross-linguistic typology is most variable. They deserve a fuller treatment, not a shorter one.

In particular, the section needs to engage with at least the following questions:

(a) If *the* has descriptively no Mod or Head distribution (modulo the marginal correlative), what synchronic property *other than* paradigmatic opposition with demonstratives anchors it in the sub-category? I gave one suggestion above (the correlative comparative); the author may have others.

(b) The phonological reduction of *the* and *a* is mentioned as evidence of grammaticalization but not as an analytic problem. Other defective members of the sub-category (the suppletive *none* for *no*, for example) are not phonologically reduced. The articles' reduction is a categorial signature of the article class cross-linguistically (see my Ch. 2.3.1.2), not an idiosyncratic English fact. It is consistent with the defective-member analysis but it doesn't follow from it.

(c) The author is honest that *every* doesn't appear in fused-head function and is therefore also defective in this way. But *every* still has Mod-like distribution (*every other day, every fifth one*) that *the* and *a* don't have. The defectiveness gradient runs *no/none > every > a > the*, and it would help the section to acknowledge this more explicitly. The articles are not just "defective members"; they are at the extreme defective end of a cline.

## Minor issues

- p. 4 (intro): "the suppletive *none* for *no* among determinatives" — this is correctly attributed to Payne 2007. Good.
- p. 6 (§3.1): The discussion of the *the lucky few* construction is excellent. I would consider giving it more prominence — perhaps a subsection of its own.
- p. 8 (§3.2): The disjunction analysis at the determiner slot — DP or genitive NP — is a real gain when collapsed to NP. I think the author could push harder on this. The fact that *Kim's*, *the*, and *my* all contribute the same kind of definiteness behaviour is genuinely surprising on a DP/NP disjunction analysis and predicted on the proposed unified NP analysis. This is one of the strongest theoretical arguments in the paper and could be foregrounded more.
- p. 9 (§4, opening paragraph): The phrase "phonologically weak, semantically minimal, and cross-linguistically non-universal" attributed to me is a fair paraphrase but not a quotation. If the author wants to use the phrasing as if it were one, it should be quoted; if it's a paraphrase, it might be worth citing the specific section (Ch. 2.3.1.2 for phonological weakness; Ch. 2.1.1 for non-universality).
- p. 11 (§4, last paragraph): "Languages vary in how far the grammaticalization cline has run and what form the endpoint takes" — yes, but this isn't quite the right framing. Languages don't all sit on the same cline. There's no single "grammaticalization cline" that all article systems travel; there are multiple grammaticalization paths converging on multiple endpoints. The article cycle I propose in Ch. 9.3.2 is one such pattern, but it's not the only one. The author should soften "the cline" to "various clines" or similar.
- The Hawaiian Creole point (Ch. 9.2.3 in my book) is interesting and relevant: *wan* (from *one*) is a new article that arose in a creole on a substrate where the English-derived *a* could have been borrowed but wasn't. This is a case where the same diachronic source produces a synchronically distinct article. It speaks to the contingency of the grammaticalization endpoint, which is relevant to the author's framing.
- The Bibliography: I cannot verify the Heine & Kuteva 2002 page references from the materials provided. I would ask the author to confirm these against the source.

## Recommendation

**Revise and resubmit.**

The core argument is strong and the paper is one of the more carefully argued contributions to this perennial debate. The two-stage argument structure (partitives establish headhood; three discriminators distinguish simple cases from Mod-Head fusion) is the right shape. The genitive simplification is a genuine theoretical gain. The handling of *every* against Spinillo is largely correct.

But the paper needs substantial revision in three areas:

1. **The English-only scope must be either justified or expanded.** The current treatment uses cross-linguistic typology selectively and disclaims it as scope. Pick one. I would prefer to see the four-noun proposal as a candidate cross-linguistic generalization with English as the test case, with at least one or two other languages briefly considered (Spanish/French as Romance test, Japanese as Type I test).

2. **§4 needs to be expanded, not compressed.** The articles are the diagnostic case, not the worst case, and they deserve a fuller treatment. In particular: a positive synchronic property of *the* and *a* that distinguishes them from elements grammaticalized out of the source category; an explicit defectiveness gradient (*no/none > every > a > the*); and a more careful disposal of the alternative reading (*the* and *a* have grammaticalized into a distinct functional category).

3. **The OE etymology in §4 needs minor correction** as detailed above. The *þ-* in modern *the* is analogical from oblique stems, not directly from OE *þæt* (which gives *that*). The author should also be careful with "distal" as a gloss for OE *se/sēo/þæt* — at the relevant historical stage this is the unmarked demonstrative, contrasting with proximal *þes/þēos/þis*.

I would also encourage the author to engage more explicitly with the broader question of what a CGEL-style category individuation requires. The paper's HPC framing is mentioned in the project notes but not foregrounded in the paper itself; if this is the larger framework the author wants the proposal to live within, it should be visible, because it bears directly on whether "defective members of a sub-category whose profile satisfies the criterion" is a coherent move (HPC theory is reasonably hospitable to such moves; classical category-individuation is much less so).

The paper should not be rejected. The core insight is correct in important respects, and a serious revision would produce a contribution worth publishing.

— C. Lyons
