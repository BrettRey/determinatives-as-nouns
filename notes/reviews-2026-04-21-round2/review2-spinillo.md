# Review: "Determinatives as nouns"

**Reviewer: Mariangela Spinillo (University of Westminster)**
**Journal: *English Language and Linguistics***

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English determinatives are best treated as a fourth sub-category of a superordinate Noun, alongside common, proper, and pronoun, retaining the determinative class as a unit; this is offered against my own positive repartitioning (Spinillo 2000, 2004), which held that the residual class of *the*, *a*, and *every* should stand apart while demonstratives and genitives migrate to the pronouns and quantifiers split across classes.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **Correct identification of the negative diagnosis.** The author rightly notes that I and they share a negative diagnosis: the inherited determiner class as a primary lexical category is not in good shape, and the territory needs to be re-described. That is the thing my 2004 thesis spent the most time on, and it is gratifying to see it taken seriously rather than dismissed in a footnote, as has too often been the case (see e.g. *CGEL* 2002, where the negative literature is not engaged at all). The author is also honest that the present proposal is meant to be a positive reconstruction of the same terrain, not just another way of saying "determiners are messy." That framing is fair.

- **The two-stage argument structure is more transparent than mine was.** I will say plainly: my 2004 thesis would have benefited from the kind of separation the author achieves between (i) the partitive headhood point and (ii) the fused-head reanalysis. Reading §3, I recognised that I had conflated the two in 2004, treating the headhood data and the redistribution argument as a single bundle. The author's separation is methodologically cleaner and worth adopting independently of whether one accepts the four-sub-category conclusion.

- **The treatment of the AUX precedent (§1) is restrained.** The Pullum & Wilson 1977 move is invoked as a sanity check rather than as an argument from analogy. I have seen this precedent abused in the dissolve-Det literature (including by sympathetic readers of my own work). The author resists that temptation. The AUX-into-V precedent does not transfer automatically, and the paper does not pretend it does.

- **The internal-modification diagnostic in §3.1 (*the lucky few*) is genuinely new to me.** I do not recall using it in 2000 or 2004, and I think it does the work the author claims: it rules out null-head and silent-NP analyses by showing there is no syntactic position left for a silent head once the determinative occupies the post-modifier slot. This is a real contribution to the dissolve-Det toolkit, irrespective of which positive reconstruction one prefers.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

### 3.1 Mischaracterisation of the 2004 repartition (§5)

The single sentence in §5 that summarises my repartition reads:

> "Spinillo offers a positive repartitioning in which demonstratives and genitives join pronouns, quantifiers split across classes, and *the*, *a*, and *every* form a residual article class."

This is approximately right but compresses three different claims into one. Two clarifications, both substantive:

1. **The 2004 thesis does not place all genitives with pronouns.** It places the *closed* genitive paradigm (*my, your, his, her, our, their, its*) with pronouns, on the basis of paradigmatic closure and dependent/independent alternation with *mine, yours,* etc. The open-class genitives (*Kim's, the king's*) remain phrasal genitives headed by the noun they morphologically attach to. The author's gloss conflates these. This matters because the present paper's §2 bridge depends on treating both kinds of genitive uniformly as "NPs headed by nouns in Det function." If the closed pronominal genitives are pronouns (as I argued and as *CGEL* also accepts), then the bridge does not need the present paper's superordinate-Noun architecture — it already runs through the existing taxonomy. The bridge is doing less work than the author thinks.

2. **The "residual article class" is not the residue left over after repartition.** It is positively defined in the 2004 thesis on three criteria: (i) restriction to Det function (no Mod, no Head use), (ii) feature-minimality (definiteness/indefiniteness only), and (iii) paradigmatic opposition along the definite/indefinite axis. *Every* is grouped with *the* and *a* not because it is "left over" but because it satisfies (i) and shares a structural property with *a* — singular-count selection without admitting Mod use of itself (\**very every student* is bad in a way \**very many students* is not). The present paper's §4 discussion of *every* (lines 197–198) does not engage with criterion (i) at all and treats the *every*-with-quantifiers move as obvious on "semantic and distributional grounds." It is not obvious. The distributional argument cuts the other way, and the semantic argument is precisely the kind of meaning-driven categorisation that *CGEL*-style methodology (and the present paper's stated commitments) are supposed to resist.

### 3.2 The argument against my repartition is rhetorical at the points where it should be substantive

Setting aside the mischaracterisation, the substantive disagreement is this: my repartition relocates problems (*the/a/every* → article class; demonstratives → pronouns; quantifiers split). The present paper's reanalysis allegedly avoids relocating any problems by keeping the class together as a sub-category of Noun. The author should ask whether the relocation is real or just hidden.

Two specific places where the relocation is hidden:

- **Demonstratives staying with determinatives requires explaining their pronominal use.** Under my account, *this* in *I want this* is straightforwardly a pronoun, parallel to *I want it*. Under the present account, *this* in *I want this* is a determinative-noun heading an NP via the simple-fused-head reanalysis. Fine, but then the author owes an account of why *this* and *that* admit the full pronominal range (deictic, anaphoric, emotive: *That's what I'm talking about*) while *some* and *every* do not. The §6 sub-cluster discussion does not address this asymmetry. My repartition explains it directly: *this/that* are pronouns; *some/every* are not. The present paper has to add sub-cluster machinery to capture what my repartition gets for free.

- **The *every* problem.** The author dismisses my placement of *every* in two sentences (§4, lines 197–198). Let me put the case more sharply than I did in 2004. *Every* is the only "quantifier" that (i) cannot be predeterminer-modified (\**all every student*, cf. *all the students, all those students*), (ii) cannot stand as a fused head (\**Every left*, conceded by the author), (iii) cannot be modified by AdvPs in the way *almost every* might suggest (the *almost* attaches to the entire NP, not the determinative — compare *almost no students*, where the same is true), and (iv) selects rigidly for singular count, which the other quantifiers do not. That is four positive features shared with *the* and *a* and not shared with the other quantifiers. The author's reply that *every* is "defective like *the* and *a*" is exactly my point — and it is the point that motivates putting them in a class together. The defectiveness pattern is not random across the determinative class; it clusters on the article-residue end. That clustering is what a positive repartition is supposed to track.

### 3.3 The "all determinatives are nouns" architecture relocates the problem rather than dissolving it

The author claims (Conclusion, §7) that the four-sub-category architecture preserves sub-cluster distinctions and "tracks what the four share." But the sub-cluster section (§6) lists three differences (AdvP modification, semantic reduction, one-Det-per-NP), and each of these maps onto a pre-existing categorial boundary. AdvP modification is a determinative-versus-the-rest fact; semantic reduction is a pronouns-and-determinatives versus common/proper-noun fact; the one-Det-per-NP restriction is a function-level constraint that is independent of category altogether. None of these are sub-cluster differences in any non-trivial sense — they are the very category boundaries the author is trying to dissolve into a supercategory.

If the sub-clusters reproduce the old categorial differences exactly, the supercategory is doing notational work, not explanatory work. The author needs to identify some shared property that holds at the supercategory level and does not reduce to the union of sub-cluster properties. NP projection alone is not enough, because NP projection is exactly the criterion under dispute — it is question-begging to use it as the supercategory glue.

This is the same complaint I would make against my own 2004 repartition in places, so I am not asking for something I have not faced myself. But the author should answer it, and the present draft does not.

### 3.4 The "defective member" move in §4 is too cheap

The author admits that *the* and *a* are "the worst case" and handles them as defective members of the determinative sub-category. The defence rests on three considerations: paradigmatic opposition, featural fit, and confinement to Det function. None of these are strong. Paradigmatic opposition is a structuralist criterion the rest of the paper does not lean on; featural fit is exactly the kind of meaning-driven argument the paper's CGEL commitments should resist; and confinement to Det function is what my 2004 thesis used to *separate* them out as a distinct class, not to assimilate them. The author has used my own positive criterion against the conclusion I drew from it without explaining why the same criterion supports a different conclusion in their hands.

If the discriminators in §3.1 (productivity, saturation, internal modification) are what makes the proposal substantive rather than notational, then *the/a/every* pass none of them, and the paper owes the reader a positive diagnostic that *the/a* satisfy and that distinguishes them from, say, prepositions or adverbs. (Pullum's review will press this same point; I press it from a different direction. The articles section is doing too much work.)

### 3.5 Cross-linguistic punt

The scope is "synchronic English" and the author flags that the cross-linguistic generalisation is left for separate work. That is reasonable as far as it goes. But the four-sub-category architecture makes a strong typological prediction — that languages with bound articles or no articles will still have a Noun supercategory with similar internal structure — and the paper should at least say what kind of evidence would falsify the claim. As it stands, the synchronic-English restriction reads more like protective hedging than principled scope-limitation.

---

## 4. Key question at Q&A

You position the paper as preferring to keep the determinative class together over my 2004 repartition because the unified treatment "does less violence to the data." But the four sub-clusters you posit in §6 reproduce, almost exactly, the categorial boundaries my repartition draws (articles vs. quantifiers vs. demonstratives, with the closed pronominal genitives separated off as pronouns). If the supercategory has to sustain these sub-cluster distinctions to be empirically adequate, in what sense is the unified architecture an improvement over the repartition, rather than the repartition wearing different labels? Specifically: name one empirical generalisation that holds at the supercategory Noun level, applies to all four sub-categories including the articles, and does not reduce to NP projection (which is the criterion under dispute).

---

## 5. Verdict

**Major revisions required.**

The paper engages with my work more carefully than most dissolve-Det discussions have done, and the methodological separation in §3 is a genuine advance. But three problems need addressing before publication:

1. The characterisation of the 2004 repartition in §5 is compressed to the point of misleading. The closed/open genitive distinction matters; the positive criteria for the article class matter; the dismissal of *every* in two sentences will not stand.

2. The defence of keeping the class together (§3.1, §6) needs to explain what work the supercategory is doing beyond renaming. As written, the sub-cluster differences in §6 reconstruct the old categorial boundaries, and the supercategory glue is NP projection, which is the disputed criterion.

3. The "defective member" treatment of *the/a* in §4 leans on criteria the rest of the paper does not endorse (paradigmatic opposition, featural fit) and uses my positive criterion (confinement to Det function) against the conclusion I drew from it, without argument.

I want to be clear that I do not regard the four-sub-category proposal as wrong on its face. It is a serious position and the author has done more to defend it than I have done to defend my own repartition since 2004. But the present draft argues from a partial reading of my work and from a sub-cluster framework that has not yet earned the right to call itself a unification. With the revisions above, the paper could be a substantive contribution to the dissolve-Det literature; without them, it will read to anyone who has worked on this terrain as a re-labelling exercise that hides the relocation of problems rather than avoiding it.

One closing note in the spirit of Rapoport's rules: the paper has improved my own thinking on the partitive-headhood/fusion separation, on the internal-modification diagnostic, and on the cleanliness of the saturation argument. I would not have written my 2004 thesis the same way after reading this draft. That is a genuine compliment, and I hope the author takes the criticisms above in the same constructive spirit.
