# Review: "Determinatives as nouns" (§§2-3)

Reviewer perspective: formal syntax in the HPSG/LFG tradition (Pollard & Sag 1994, Netter 1994, Allegranza 1998, Van Eynde 2003/2007, Payne, Huddleston & Pullum 2007).

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that the so-called simple fused-head uses of determinatives (*some left*, *many came*) are not fusion at all but canonical headedness, and that once this reanalysis is granted, the determiner function is uniformly filled by NP; the case rests on two positive discriminators (paradigm productivity within the determinative class, and structural saturation by the determinative alone) that purport to distinguish simple cases from the Mod-Head residue (*the rich*, *the poor*).

## 2. Strengths

1. **The discriminators are the right shape.** Paradigm productivity (a broad lexical sweep across the class) and structural saturation (sole-element NPs in argument function) are exactly the kinds of positive evidence an HPSG grammarian would want before positing a construction-specific mechanism like FF. Payne, Huddleston & Pullum 2007 (pp. 569-70) themselves stress that FF is expected to be "restricted in its application within a given language to a small set of constructions," which is implicitly an argument from narrow lexicalisation. If a pattern is broadly productive across a class, that cuts against the construction-specific apparatus. You've turned PHP's own expectation against the CGEL treatment of simple cases - nicely done, and not something PHP 2007 confront directly.

2. **The "Det = NP" collapse in §3.2 is theoretically attractive.** Making the determiner function homogeneous in its category is a real unification, not just a relabeling. On standard HPSG assumptions, a functor (SPR/SELECT) that can take genitive NPs, determinative phrases, and PPs like *up to two* as values is doing more work than one that just takes NPs. The uniform-NP-filler proposal aligns with Van Eynde 2003, 2007 in spirit (noun is head, prenominal dependents are functors), and makes a clean prediction: the article-blocking and fused-head-licensing patterns should be properties of the slot, not of disjoint filler-type machinery. The three downstream observations in §3.2 (definiteness contribution, article blocking, fused-head licensing) are genuinely accounted for.

3. **The Mod-Head residue is handled honestly.** You don't try to absorb *the rich* into the new story. That restraint is important: keeping FF for cases where it demonstrably earns its keep (AdjP with a determiner requirement, narrow lexical base, no productivity) preserves the apparatus where the empirical profile actually fits, and makes the residue argument falsifiable rather than rhetorical.

## 3. Weaknesses (specific and actionable)

1. **The saturation discriminator quietly begs the question against an empty-N analysis.** Your saturation argument runs: *Some left* is a full NP with *some* as the sole element, therefore no fusion. But the very same data is compatible with at least three other analyses: (a) CGEL's FF account (*some* fuses Det+Head); (b) an empty-N / pro-NP analysis (null head with *some* in Det); (c) your determinative-as-N analysis. Saturation - the observation that no further structure is needed - doesn't discriminate between them; all three analyses deliver a single-word NP. What you actually need is evidence that the single word is the head by independent tests (agreement, selection, inflection, coordination), not just evidence that the NP is saturated. PHP 2007 footnote 5 explicitly argue that adjacency and distribution favour FF over a null-head analysis; you need to engage that argument on its own terms. Action: either (i) add a positive test for head status (e.g., agreement mismatch à la Van Eynde 2007: *these* triggers plural agreement as head of an NP with no further noun - *these are mine*), or (ii) relabel the section as eliminating the *apparatus* rather than the *structure* (which would be a weaker but defensible claim).

2. **Paradigm productivity cuts both ways, and the defective set is larger than you acknowledge.** You say "the pattern remains broadly productive across the rest of the class" after listing *the*, *a*, *every*, and *no* as defective. But those four include the two most frequent determinatives in English by a wide margin (*the* is the most common word in English; *a* is in the top ten). By token frequency, the "defective" set dominates. Your response - that defective members belong on paradigmatic grounds (cf. *the Hague* among proper nouns) - is principled, but the parallel isn't quite right: *the Hague* is lexically idiosyncratic within a productive class, whereas the defectiveness of *the* and *a* is categorial (no member of the article sub-class fused-heads). PHP 2007 (pp. 371-2) note this same asymmetry but draw the opposite conclusion: that the articles' failure to fused-head is evidence the fused use is constructionally constrained. You need to explain why categorial (not idiosyncratic) defectiveness in the highest-frequency members should be treated as paradigmatic gap rather than as evidence for a sub-class-specific distributional pattern. Action: address §4 (the articles section, currently TODO) head-on, and consider whether the articles warrant a separate sub-sub-category of determinative-nouns - defective for the head-of-NP property but sharing the determiner-function profile.

3. **§3.2's "Det = NP" claim is under-specified on the selection mechanism.** In HPSG, the question isn't just "what category fills Det?" but "what features does the head noun require of its specifier?" On a standard Pollard & Sag / Van Eynde treatment, nouns lexically select a determiner with particular features (definite, count, number-agreement). If Det is uniformly NP, then the head's SPR / SELECT value must be sensitive to internal properties of the NP filler (is it a determinative-headed NP, a genitive NP, or a plain NP like *what size*?). The disjunction doesn't actually disappear - it re-emerges inside the NP selection requirements. This is a real problem for the "simplification" claim. Van Eynde 2007 (§2.2) handles this by letting the functor select its head sister via SELECT, with features distinguishing article-like, demonstrative-like, and genitive-NP-like functors. Under your proposal, where all these are NPs, the selection mechanism has to reach inside the NP to the head's sub-category. That's not necessarily bad, but it's not free. Action: say explicitly what features on the head-N distinguish an NP-in-Det from an NP-in-Subject/Object. Without this, the §3.2 argument is under-determined between "real structural simplification" and "the disjunction moved one level down."

## 4. Key question I would ask at Q&A

If *some* in *some left* is the canonical head of the NP (a determinative-noun), then the NP *some* should have the distributional profile of an NP headed by a determinative-noun in every other respect - not just saturation and argument position. So: what is the agreement profile of determinative-headed NPs? *These are mine*, *all are welcome*, *none are missing/is missing* all show plural (or variable) agreement that must be inherited from somewhere. On your proposal, does the determinative-noun lexically specify number, or does it inherit number from a covert associate (anaphoric construal)? If the former, you owe us a paradigm for *some* (presumably uncountable-singular-but-also-plural?). If the latter, you have quietly smuggled in the anaphoric mechanism that PHP 2007 use to justify the fused-head analysis in the first place.

## 5. Verdict

**Revise - the §3.1 discriminator argument is publishable with repairs, but §3.2 needs a concrete selection story before the Det=NP claim earns its keep.** Productivity is a genuinely strong discriminator; saturation is weaker than it looks and needs a head-identification test to do the work you want.
