# Review: "Determinatives as nouns" (§2 and §3)

**Reviewer perspective:** Generativist syntactician working within the DP hypothesis tradition (Abney 1987, Bernstein, Larson). Skeptical of the CGEL category/function distinction and of NP-over-DP analyses. Reviewing §2 (Bridge from Noun to Determiner) and §3 (The category-membership argument, including §3.1 Fused-head reanalysis and §3.2 Det uniformly filled by NP).

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that determinatives are a sub-category of Noun on the strength of two bridging observations (paradigmatic alignment between *my/mine* and the fact that genitive NPs fill determiner function) and a reanalysis of CGEL's simple fused-head constructions as canonical N-headed NPs, so that items like *some* in *I'll take some* project an NP exactly parallel to *I'll take dogs*.

---

## 2. Charitable re-expression (Rapoport's Rules)

Before criticism, let me state the proposal as the author would endorse it.

The proposal is not Abney's DP hypothesis in disguise. The author accepts that *the dog* is an NP with a DP in determiner function, keeping the category/function distinction sharp. The novel claim is taxonomic: the four CGEL-style classes (common N, proper N, pronoun, determinative) are not four co-ordinate primary categories but four sub-clusters under a single superordinate Noun. The machinery defending the claim is a profile-level membership criterion: a sub-category counts as nominal iff its items canonically head NPs in subject/object/PrepC function, where "canonically" means Head is part of the sub-category's profile rather than reached via fusion.

The §2 bridge is genuinely a softening move, not an argument for membership. It shows (a) that CGEL already splits an aligned possessive paradigm (*my*/*mine*) across two of its primary classes, creating an anomaly that the sub-category move would dissolve, and (b) that CGEL already admits NP-headed constituents (genitive NPs) into Det function. The author is honest that §2 alone doesn't establish the membership claim.

The §3.1 fused-head reanalysis is the load-bearing argument. It offers two discriminators (paradigm productivity and structural saturation) that distinguish simple fused-head from Mod-Head fusion without presupposing the conclusion. Productivity: ~14 determinatives admit fused-head use, vs. a small, lexically restricted set of adjectives. Saturation: *Some left* is a complete NP; *Rich get richer* (intended as class-referring) isn't. The inference is that simple "fused-heads" are just canonical heads, and fusion machinery should be reserved for the genuine residue (Mod-Head in *the rich*).

§3.2 cashes in a downstream payoff: once determinatives are nouns, Det function is uniformly filled by NP, and the disjunctive "DP-or-genitive-NP" specification collapses. The unified account explains (it is claimed) parallel definiteness, article-blocking, and fused-head licensing across the two filler types.

There is genuine craft here. The author takes the AUX-in-V precedent seriously as a sanity check (not an argument), distinguishes the productive and lexicalized cases cleanly, and reserves fusion-of-functions machinery for where it earns its keep. The paper is not trying to win by elimination.

---

## 3. Strengths

**(a) The productivity/saturation asymmetry is a real observation.** The author is right that simple fused-head and Mod-Head fusion don't pattern alike. On a conservative CGEL analysis this asymmetry is present but unexplained: both are "fusion of functions," yet one is broadly productive and self-saturating, the other narrowly lexicalized and parasitic on a determiner. Even a reviewer hostile to the conclusion should concede that the asymmetry demands an account. The author's diagnosis — that these aren't the same construction — deserves engagement.

**(b) The *my/mine* paradigmatic split is embarrassing for the standard CGEL taxonomy.** On the existing view, two near-completely aligned inflectional sets sit in two unrelated primary categories. Brett has pressed this in earlier work (Reynolds 2013, 2014), and §2 restates it crisply. A DP-theorist can reply that this is an argument about category labels, not about whether D heads its own projection, but the local tension inside CGEL is not manufactured.

**(c) The author resists overreach.** §3.1 explicitly keeps Mod-Head fusion for *the rich* class, §2 brackets off cardinal numerals (deferring to Reynolds 2026), and §2 is explicit that the bridge is softening rather than probative. Precedent (AUX-in-V) is flagged as a sanity check, not an argument from analogy. This is the kind of author who will not claim more than they've shown, which makes the core argument easier to take seriously.

---

## 4. Weaknesses (from a DP-hypothesis defender's stance)

**(a) The "saturation" discriminator equivocates between semantics and syntax.** In §3.1 the contrast between *Some left* and *\*Rich get richer* is presented as a structural fact (the determinative "saturates" the NP; the AdjP doesn't). But on a DP analysis, exactly the expected generalisation is that functional heads select and saturate nominal projections — *some* projects a DP without overt NP complement (NP elided, pro, or semantically type-shifted), while *rich* is a lexical A that doesn't. The author's data are consistent with this DP account and with the author's proposal; they don't discriminate. What looks like "canonical headedness" here is ambiguous between (i) *some* is an N, and (ii) *some* is a D selecting a null/elided NP. §3.1 needs a diagnostic that rules out the DP reading — e.g., something that shows *some* patterns with common nouns rather than with functional heads under movement, binding, agreement, or ellipsis licensing. Productivity and saturation are symptoms of being a *head*; they don't tell us whether that head is lexical N or functional D.

**(b) The membership criterion is question-begging at the profile level.** §3 defines the criterion as: "items of C can head NPs filling subject/object/PrepC function, where Head is part of the category's profile rather than a function reached via fusion." But whether the relevant occurrences are fusion vs. canonical is exactly what §3.1 is arguing about. The criterion tells us that if you reanalyse the fusion cases as canonical, then determinatives qualify as nouns. That's a conditional, not an argument. The reviewer wants: an independent reason to think simple fused-heads are canonical that doesn't already presuppose the reanalysis. The productivity argument is the best candidate, but it's a frequency/lexicalization observation, not a structural one. The saturation argument is (a) above. Without an independent structural diagnostic, the criterion is a definition tuned to admit the conclusion.

**(c) §3.2's "three observations" don't discriminate between the proposal and a DP account (or even the status quo).** Shared definiteness across *the book*/*Kim's book*, uniform article-blocking, and parallel fused-head licensing all fall out of DP just as easily: both fillers occupy SpecDP or D itself and contribute to definiteness at the DP level; one-D-per-DP follows from the functional head being singular; fused-head licensing is ellipsis of the NP complement. The author presents §3.2 as cashing a consequence of §3.1, but the three observations are theory-neutral generalisations that any reasonable account captures. The DP theorist will read §3.2 as noting facts a DP analysis already explains. What §3.2 owes is a comparison: in what way does the NP-collapse account make different predictions from a DP account, and are those predictions borne out?

**Secondary issue — the AUX-in-V precedent is weaker than advertised.** The author explicitly calls it a "sanity check, not an argument," which I'll accept. But note that auxiliaries still exhibit a cluster of non-verbal properties (NICE, defective paradigms) that CGEL handles as sub-category-level facts. The parallel actually helps the DP theorist: just as NICE properties motivated a distinct AUX category for many researchers before Pullum & Wilson, the closed-class, functional, cross-linguistically recurrent properties of D motivate a distinct category on the DP view. The precedent cuts both ways.

---

## 5. Key question (Q&A)

"Your two discriminators — paradigm productivity and structural saturation — show that simple fused-head constructions don't pattern with Mod-Head fusion. Granted. But neither discriminator distinguishes your proposal (determinatives are lexical Ns that canonically head NPs) from a DP analysis (determinatives are functional Ds that take null or elided NP complements, with the functional head licensing the saturation). Productivity reflects the closed functional class; saturation reflects functional headedness plus NP-ellipsis licensing. Can you offer a diagnostic — binding, agreement, movement, ellipsis parallels, cross-linguistic alignment, or acquisition evidence — that determinatives behave like lexical N heads specifically, rather than like functional heads with a silent complement? Without such a diagnostic, §3.1 reads as compatible with the DP hypothesis, not an argument against it."

---

## 6. Verdict

**Major revision.** The paper identifies a real asymmetry and a real paradigmatic tension inside CGEL, but §3.1's positive argument doesn't discriminate its N-analysis from a functional-D analysis of the same data, and §3.2's downstream "simplification" is theory-neutral. The proposal needs at least one structural diagnostic that cuts specifically between "lexical N head" and "functional D head with silent complement" before the categorial conclusion follows.
