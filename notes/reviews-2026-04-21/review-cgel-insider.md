# CGEL-Insider Review of "Determinatives as Nouns"

*Reviewer persona: Senior CGEL-trained linguist (Huddleston/Pullum tradition), expertise in English syntax, lexical categories, category/function distinction, fused-head constructions, CGEL Chapters 5 and 17.*

*Note: TODO placeholders in §1, §4, §5, §6, §7 are not evaluated. This review covers §2 (Bridge) and §3 (Category-membership argument, §3.1 fused-head reanalysis, §3.2 determiner slot uniformity).*

---

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues that English determinatives should be reclassified as a fourth sub-category of a superordinate Noun category (alongside common nouns, proper nouns, and pronouns), grounding the reclassification in a reanalysis of simple fused-head constructions as canonical headedness of an NP by a determinative-noun, while preserving CGEL's category/function distinction and the N-headed NP analysis.

---

## 2. Strengths

- **The fused-head reanalysis is well-structured, with two independent discriminators (paradigm productivity vs Mod-Head's lexical restriction; structural saturation vs Mod-Head's requirement for a determiner) that don't beg the categorial question.** The Mod-Head contrast is particularly effective: the asymmetry between broadly productive determinative fused-head uses and the small class-of-people adjective set (\textit{the rich, the poor, the elderly}) is a genuine empirical handle, and the saturation test (*\textit{Rich get richer}* vs \textit{Some left}) is a neat demonstration that the two phenomena differ structurally. This is sharper than the older move of just asserting that fused-head is "really" headedness.

- **The paradigmatic-relatedness point in §2 (\textit{my/mine, your/yours}) is a real pressure point on CGEL's existing taxonomy.** Splitting an aligned inflectional paradigm across two unrelated primary categories (determinative vs pronoun) is genuinely awkward, and the author is right that it's "accommodated but not predicted" under the status quo. The observation is old (Reynolds 2013, 2014), but framing it as a bridge to the superordinate-Noun claim (rather than just an oddity) gives it more argumentative purchase.

- **The §3.2 downstream simplification — "Det = NP" uniformly — is genuinely attractive.** The disjunctive Det filler (DP or genitive NP) under CGEL is an accepted but un-explained feature of the Ch 5 architecture. Collapsing it to a single filler category does have predictive bite on the three patterns listed (shared definiteness contribution, uniform article blocking, fused-head licensing across both filler types). The move is careful: the author flags that this is a \textit{consequence} of §3.1 rather than an independent argument, which is the right epistemic posture.

---

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The Pullum & Wilson (1977) AUX-into-V analogy in §2 is dangerously load-bearing, and the "sanity check" disclaimer doesn't fully insulate it.** AUX-in-V rests on distributional evidence that modal/primary auxiliaries share core verb properties (tense inflection, subject agreement, VP-projection) that determinatives manifestly don't share with common nouns (no number inflection on \textit{the} or \textit{every}, no count/mass contrast, no pluralization, no modification by AdjPs in the usual way). The analogy risks suggesting a parallel that isn't fully earned. The author should either (a) show, concretely, that determinatives share N-level core properties with common nouns (at the supercategory level, not just via fused-head headedness), or (b) drop the AUX analogy and lean on the HPC framing from the book (sub-clusters of an HPC kind needn't share all profile properties; they share a central tendency). The current framing borrows the authority of the AUX precedent without doing the comparative work.

- **The membership criterion in §3 is under-specified in one crucial respect: what exactly counts as "part of the sub-category's profile" vs "a function reached via fusion"?** The criterion is stated as "Head is part of the category's profile rather than a function reached via fusion with another function," and the two discriminators (productivity, saturation) are offered to distinguish the determinative case from Mod-Head. But the criterion itself presupposes a principled answer to when a distributional pattern is profile-level vs fusion-level. Consider: proper nouns typically don't take determiners (\textit{Kim left}, *\textit{the Kim left}), but some (\textit{the Hague, the Bronx, the United States}) require one. Is article-requiring a proper-noun-profile property, or is it handled via fusion, lexical idiosyncrasy, or sub-sub-categorization? The paper gestures at "defective members are admitted on paradigmatic grounds" but doesn't spell out how to apply this principle in contested cases. The determinative class has lexically defective members (\textit{the, a, every}) the author admits can't head NPs; what prevents a reviewer from saying the entire class is defective in the same way, i.e., the productive fused-head pattern is itself a fusion? The productivity discriminator pushes back, but the principled distinction between "broadly productive fusion" and "canonical headedness" needs more argumentative cash-out than §3.1 provides.

- **The Table 1 presentation elides a real distributional asymmetry that CGEL insiders will notice immediately.** The four sub-categories are shown heading NPs in subject, object, and PrepC function with one-cell examples each. But determinative-headed NPs are systematically \textit{restricted} in ways the other three sub-categories aren't. \textit{Some left} is fine, but \textit{Some book is missing} (with \textit{some} heading the NP, not in Det function) is bad on that reading. The NP-heading uses of determinatives are overwhelmingly anaphoric/elliptical in interpretation (\textit{some} = \textit{some of the apples mentioned}), whereas \textit{Dogs bark} and \textit{Kim left} aren't. CGEL's fusion analysis is motivated precisely by this anaphoric dependency. The reanalysis needs to address: if determinatives canonically head NPs, why are those NPs almost always interpreted with respect to a salient antecedent set, in a way that common-noun-headed and proper-noun-headed NPs aren't? The anaphoric dependency doesn't falsify the reanalysis, but it's a property that needs to show up in the determinative-noun sub-cluster's profile; it's currently absent from the argument.

---

## 4. Key Q&A question

*"Your §3.1 reanalysis treats \textit{Some left} and \textit{Dogs bark} as structurally parallel, differing only in which sub-category of N heads the NP. But \textit{some} in that use is obligatorily anaphoric — it picks up a contextually salient set — while \textit{dogs} is not. Under CGEL's current analysis, that anaphoric dependency falls out of the head-function being anaphorically construed under fusion. Under your analysis, where does the anaphoric dependency live? Is it a property of the determinative-noun sub-cluster's profile (in which case your Table 1 is missing a diagnostic column), a property of the specific lexemes, or something the construction inherits from a different source? And: does the same story cover \textit{I'll take Kim's} (the genitive NP case in §3.2), which also looks anaphoric?"*

---

## 5. Verdict

**Revise & Resubmit.**

The core reanalysis (§3.1) is strong enough to warrant a serious second look — the two-discriminator argument against treating simple fused-head as genuine fusion is the best version of this move I've seen in print. But the paper as currently drafted has a gap at the criterion-specification level and an unaddressed asymmetry (the anaphoric-dependency property of determinative-headed NPs) that competent CGEL reviewers will land on immediately. The §2 bridge also leans on an AUX analogy that works less well than the author claims. These are fixable with revision; none of them are fatal to the thesis. The TODO sections (§1, §4, §5, §6, §7) will matter more than the author may realize: §4 on articles (the \textit{the}/\textit{a} defectiveness) and §5 on nearby proposals (Postal, Payne & Huddleston, the DP literature) are where reviewers will look to see whether the author has done the comparative-analysis work the current draft promises.
