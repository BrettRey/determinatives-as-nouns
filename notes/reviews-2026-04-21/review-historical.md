# Review: "Determinatives as nouns" (historical-linguistics perspective)

Reviewer frame: grammaticalization of determiners (Heine & Kuteva 2002 *World Lexicon of Grammaticalization*; Diessel 1999 *Demonstratives*; Lyons 1999 *Definiteness*; Greenberg 1978 on article cycles).

## 1. One-sentence summary

Reynolds argues that English determinatives (*the, a, this, some, every, my*) should be treated as a fourth sub-category of **Noun** alongside common nouns, proper nouns, and pronouns, supported by (i) paradigmatic and structural bridges already in CGEL (§2) and (ii) a positive criterion of canonical NP-headhood that determinatives meet once so-called fused-head uses are reanalysed as ordinary N-heading (§3).

## 2. Strengths

1. **The §3 positive criterion is the right shape for a categorial argument.** Requiring that Head function be part of the *sub-category profile* rather than reached via fusion, and grounding the test in distribution (subject/object/PrepC), is methodologically conservative and avoids semantic circularity. The two discriminators (paradigm productivity vs. lexical listing; structural saturation without a licensing Det) do genuine work: they pick out the simple fused-head cases as distinct from Mod-Head AdjP fusion (*the rich*) without presupposing the conclusion. This is the kind of test a diachronician can also apply cross-linguistically, because productivity and saturation are observable.

2. **The §2 bridge via genitive NPs is well-grounded and historically resonant.** The observation that genitive NPs regularly fill determiner function (*Kim's book*, *the king's every whim*, *that man next door's car*) is a genuine structural link between the noun system and the determiner slot. Historically this is well-motivated: the English genitive *'s* is itself a former case suffix reanalysed as a phrasal clitic (Allen 2008 *Genitives in Early English*), and across Germanic the determiner slot and the genitive-NP slot have interacted repeatedly. The paper's point that "Det = NP" unifies two currently disjunctive fillers is a real simplification, not just relabelling.

3. **The AUX-in-V precedent (Pullum & Wilson 1977) is apt and honestly deployed.** Flagged explicitly as a sanity check rather than an argument from analogy, this is exactly the right use of precedent: a demonstration that the same descriptive tradition already absorbs a closed functional class into an open lexical category when the distributional case warrants it. It also inoculates the reader against the reflex that "closed class = separate category."

## 3. Weaknesses

1. **The diachronic dimension is entirely absent, and that's a liability given the thesis.** The paper argues synchronically that determinatives are nouns, but it never engages the overwhelming cross-linguistic evidence about *where determinatives come from*. Heine & Kuteva (2002) document the canonical paths DEMONSTRATIVE > DEFINITE ARTICLE and ONE / INDEFINITE PRONOUN > INDEFINITE ARTICLE (Greenberg's article cycle; Diessel 1999 Ch. 6). In English specifically, *the* descends from the OE distal demonstrative *se/sēo/þæt* (itself arguably a pronoun/determinative in CGEL terms), and *a(n)* from OE *ān* 'one' (numeral/quantifier). Pronouns and demonstratives are frequent diachronic sources for determinatives; common nouns are a well-attested source for *classifiers* and sometimes for articles (Greenberg's Stage 0 → Stage I). This is a gift to the paper's argument: the diachronic sources of English determinatives are already nominal (demonstratives, numerals, pronouns), which supports the synchronic superordinate-Noun grouping. The author should say so. Not mentioning this evidence leaves the paper looking like it doesn't know the typological literature, and it also misses the single cleanest piece of external support for the HPC-style sub-clustering story (origins → mechanisms → stabilised sub-profile).

2. **The "defective members" strategy for *the* and *a* needs a diachronic backstop or it risks circularity.** The promise in §3 is that *the* and *a* are admitted on paradigmatic grounds as defective members of a sub-category whose profile satisfies the criterion, parallel to *the Hague* among proper nouns. The parallel is strained. *The Hague* is defective in requiring an article; it still heads an NP and refers. *The* and *a* never head NPs in any construction (not even fused-head, as §3 explicitly concedes via CGEL and Payne). The paradigm-membership rescue therefore relies entirely on treating them as the grammaticalised end of a continuum whose other members meet the criterion. Historically that's correct (articles are the phonologically weak, semantically minimal limit of the determinative/demonstrative/numeral cline; Lyons 1999 Ch. 9; Diessel 1999), but if the synchronic criterion says "heads NPs in canonical functions" and these items *don't*, the move from "most members satisfy" to "the whole class is N" needs a principled defectivity story. "Defective proper noun *the Hague*" isn't that story because *the Hague* still heads. The paper needs either (a) a sharper account of which kinds of paradigmatic defectiveness preserve sub-category membership (with *the Hague* and *the/a* on the same side and something diagnostic on the other) or (b) explicit appeal to grammaticalisation: *the* and *a* are category members by descent and by continued paradigmatic contrast, even though morphosyntactic attrition has stripped the distributional property that motivates the sub-category. An HPC-style "cluster membership via enough properties, not all" frame would help here, but it isn't deployed in §3.

3. **The cross-linguistic generalisation of the claim is untested and the paper would benefit from saying so up front.** The argument runs on English data and on CGEL's specific architecture. But "Determinative is a sub-category of Noun" is the kind of claim readers will want to apply or resist cross-linguistically, and many languages have determiner-like items that clearly aren't nominal (verbal classifiers, preposed particles, affixal articles as in Bulgarian, Romanian, Scandinavian). Even within Germanic, the Scandinavian suffixal definite article (*hus-et* 'house-the') looks like a bound morpheme, not a noun. The paper should either scope the claim explicitly to English (and say why English is representative or special) or give typological guidance on how the criterion transfers. Without that, §3's criterion could be read as smuggling English-specific structural facts (NPs with overt determiners, no bound articles) into a definition of nounhood.

## 4. Key question at Q&A

> Given that English *the* descends from the Old English distal demonstrative *þæt/se/sēo* and *a(n)* from the numeral *ān* — both sources that your own analysis already treats as nouns (demonstratives are determinative-nouns; numerals are absorbed into existing sub-categories per Reynolds 2026) — would you commit to the stronger diachronic claim that the articles are **category members by descent** from the determinative sub-cluster, with their synchronic defectiveness the endpoint of grammaticalisation rather than evidence against membership? If so, what distinguishes this appeal to descent from circularity, given that the synchronic criterion in §3 is distributional? And does the criterion need an HPC-style "enough properties, not all" qualifier to admit *the* and *a* non-circularly?

## 5. Verdict

**Revise and resubmit — the synchronic argument in §§2-3 is well-constructed and largely convincing, but the paper is incomplete without engagement with the grammaticalisation literature, which is both the most serious omission and, ironically, the strongest external support for the thesis.**
