# Review: "Determinatives as nouns" (Reynolds)

Reviewer perspective: usage-based / construction grammar (Croft, Goldberg, Haspelmath). Sceptical of primary lexical categories and of the category/function distinction.

## 1. One-sentence summary

The paper argues, within CGEL's architecture, that English determinatives satisfy a head-NP membership criterion and should therefore be reclassified as a sub-category of a supercategory Noun, alongside common nouns, proper nouns, and pronouns.

## 2. Strengths

- **The fused-head discriminators are genuinely well-chosen.** The two-pronged argument in §3.1, paradigm productivity (broad across the determinative class, narrowly lexicalised for adjectival Mod-Head like *the rich*) and structural saturation (*Some left* vs. \*Rich get richer*), identifies a real distributional asymmetry that any theory has to reckon with. Even a constructionist who rejects the "canonical Head" framing has to account for why *some*/*many*/*all* saturate NP slots productively while *rich*/*poor* don't. That's a useful finding regardless of the category-theoretic packaging.
- **The "bridge" in §2 is rhetorically effective and empirically honest.** The paradigmatic my/mine split (straddling the determinative/pronoun line) and the NP-in-Det-function point about genitive NPs (*Kim's book*, *the king's every whim*) are exactly the kind of facts that expose the arbitrariness of the incumbent taxonomy. I learned from the *CGEL*-internal observation that the determiner slot is already disjunctively filled by two different phrase types, and the downstream simplification in §3.2 (Det = NP uniformly) is a clean structural pay-off that even a non-CGEL reader can appreciate.
- **The Pullum & Wilson (1977) AUX-in-V precedent is a fair sanity check, not an argument from analogy.** The paper is careful to frame it that way (§2, final paragraph). Within the CGEL descriptive tradition, the precedent is real: a closed functional class was absorbed into an open lexical category without architectural collapse. That's disciplined use of intra-framework precedent.

## 3. Weaknesses

- **The membership criterion is methodologically opportunistic in exactly Croft's (2001) sense.** The criterion is "heads NPs in subject, object, PrepC positions, canonically rather than via fusion." But "canonically rather than via fusion" is where all the work is being done, and the discriminator for canonical-vs-fusion (§3.1) is itself selected to draw the line where the author wants it drawn. Paradigm productivity and structural saturation are real asymmetries, but why these two discriminators and not, e.g., inflectional morphology (where determinatives fail the noun test: no plural *\*somes*, no genitive *\*some's*), modification potential (*very* *few* but not *very dogs*), or coordination with canonical nouns (*\*the dogs and some*)? The paper owes the reader an argument that the chosen discriminators aren't just those that happen to put determinatives inside N while keeping AdjP Mod-Head outside. Without that, the criterion looks engineered to reach a conclusion rather than applied to test one. This is the exact pathology Croft diagnosed.
- **The "HPC sub-cluster" framing (mentioned in CLAUDE.md, presumably intended for §6) has to do more than label the result.** From a constructionist angle, the superordinate-Noun claim cuts both ways. If determinatives, pronouns, common nouns, and proper nouns all share a "Noun profile," the paper needs to state the profile (which properties cluster, with what stabilising mechanisms) and show that the four sub-categories share it more tightly than they share properties with, say, numerals, quantifier adjectives (*numerous*, *countless*), or classifier-like nouns (*kind*, *sort*, *type*). Right now the four-way taxonomy is stipulated in the abstract and bridged to in §2 without any independent test that the cluster boundary actually falls where the paper claims. A usage-based reader will ask: how do you know *some* is closer to *dog* than to *numerous*? The draft doesn't say.
- **The category/function distinction does unexplained theoretical work throughout §2-§3.** The argument repeatedly leans on formulations like "Head is part of the sub-category's profile rather than a function reached via fusion" (§3, §3.1) and "the specification 'Det = NP' covers all the cases" (§3.2). Both rely on treating Head and Det as functions distinct from the categories that realise them. A constructionist would ask: what independent evidence is there for functions as a level of representation separate from the constructions that pair form and meaning? The *CGEL* architecture is being presupposed, not defended, and the paper's conclusion depends on that architecture. The membership criterion only has bite because "canonical Head" is a coherent notion; on a construction-grammar view, there are just constructions like [Det N], [Det + ellipsis anaphora], [Adj + class-reference] — each with its own productivity profile, no taxonomy required to predict the saturation asymmetry. The paper should either defend the architecture or acknowledge that the argument is conditional on it.

## 4. Key question for Q&A

You've chosen paradigm productivity and structural saturation as the diagnostics that distinguish "canonical Head" from "Head via fusion." These discriminators happen to split the data exactly where you need them to — determinatives inside N, adjectival Mod-Head outside. What test did you consider and reject? More pointedly: if we applied inflectional morphology (plural, genitive) or pre-modification by degree adverbs as the discriminator instead, determinatives would fail and adjectives would partly pass. Why are productivity and saturation the right diagnostics for nounhood, and not the morphological ones that a typologist would reach for first?

## 5. Verdict

Revise and resubmit, conditionally. The §3.1 asymmetry is a real finding and worth publishing; the superordinate-Noun reclassification is a live question; but the membership criterion needs to be defended against the opportunism charge before the reclassification claim is convincing, and the framework-dependence of the argument needs acknowledgement so non-CGEL readers can locate the load-bearing assumptions.
